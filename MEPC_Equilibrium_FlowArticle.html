Act One

The System — Theory, Law, and Principle

Everything wants to reach the most stable, lowest-energy state possible. Equilibrium is what happens when two competing forces find the exact point where neither can win anymore — and then keep fighting anyway, at a standstill.

The opening compression
Equilibrium is not stillness. It is a tie — both reactions running at equal rates, the tide coming in exactly as fast as it goes out, the sandcastle being built and destroyed simultaneously at the same point.

The theoretical foundation is the Second Law of Thermodynamics: the universe moves toward maximum disorder. But reactions are not just about disorder. They are also about energy — specifically, whether releasing heat makes the process more or less favourable. Gibbs Free Energy (ΔG) combines both tendencies into a single verdict: will this happen, and how far will it go?

When ΔG = 0, neither forward nor reverse reaction is favoured. The system is at equilibrium. That condition gives rise to the equilibrium constant K — a precise number that tells you exactly where the balance point sits.

Gibbs Free Energy — what drives every reaction to its equilibrium

The equilibrium constant K is born from ΔG. Every time you write a K expression, you are encoding the exact energy balance of a reaction into a ratio of concentrations or pressures. The number K tells you everything about where the system wants to end up — but nothing about how fast it gets there. Kinetics and equilibrium are separate conversations.

Hess's Law — the foundation
The total enthalpy change of a reaction is independent of the path taken. Only the start and end points matter. Whether you break the journey into two steps or twenty, the total ΔH is identical — which means you can calculate the heat of any reaction without measuring it directly.

Le Chatelier's Principle is the practical arm of equilibrium theory. Disturb a system at equilibrium — change concentration, pressure, or temperature — and the system shifts to oppose the disturbance. It is not a law in the strict thermodynamic sense; it is a predictive principle derived from the fact that K is a fixed number at a given temperature. If you add a reactant, Q drops below K, so the system shifts forward to restore K. Simple as the tide adjusting to the moon.

  • Increase concentration of reactant — Q < K → shifts forward → more products form until Q = K again.
  • Increase concentration of product — Q > K → shifts backward → reactants reform until Q = K.
  • Increase pressure (gases) — shifts toward the side with fewer moles of gas. Fewer molecules means less pressure — the system resists the change.
  • Increase temperature — shifts in the endothermic direction. The system absorbs the extra heat to resist the increase. K itself changes — unlike concentration or pressure changes.
  • Add a catalyst — equilibrium position does NOT change. K does NOT change. Only the rate at which equilibrium is reached increases. JAMB tests this every year.
The critical distinction
Concentration and pressure changes shift the position of equilibrium but do not change K. Temperature changes actually change K. A catalyst changes neither K nor equilibrium position — only speed. These three facts are three of the most tested ideas in JAMB chemistry.
Act Two

The Symbols — Every K Decoded

The K family is not one constant — it is a type system. Each member applies to a specific situation: solutions, gases, water, acids, bases, sparingly soluble salts. The notation carries the meaning. Read the subscript; know the context.

The Act 2 compression
Every K expression is just products over reactants, raised to their stoichiometric powers, with solids and pure liquids excluded. That one rule, applied to different contexts, generates the entire K family.
The K family — one rule, many contexts

The fundamental rule for writing any K expression: products over reactants, raised to stoichiometric powers, pure solids and pure liquids excluded. This one rule generates every K expression you will ever write. The subscript changes; the logic does not.

SymbolFull nameUsesUnitJAMB trap
Kc Concentration equilibrium constant [products]^p / [reactants]^r mol/dm³ raised to Δn — or dimensionless by convention Powers come from coefficients, not molecular formula subscripts. 2A ⇌ B → Kc = [B]/[A]²
Kp Pressure equilibrium constant P(products)^p / P(reactants)^r atm or Pa raised to Δn Only gaseous species appear. Solids and liquids excluded same as Kc.
Kw Ionic product of water [H⁺][OH⁻] = 1×10⁻¹⁴ at 25°C mol²/dm⁶ Kw increases with temperature — water ionises more at higher T. Neutral pH is NOT always 7.
Ka Acid dissociation constant [H⁺][A⁻] / [HA] mol/dm³ Large Ka = strong acid. pKa = −log Ka, so large Ka = small pKa. Inverse relationship.
Kb Base dissociation constant [BH⁺][OH⁻] / [B] mol/dm³ Ka × Kb = Kw. If you know Ka of the acid, you know Kb of its conjugate base.
Ksp Solubility product [cation]^a × [anion]^b varies with formula The solid itself is excluded. AB → Ksp = [A⁺][B⁻] = s². But A₂B → Ksp = [A⁺]²[B²⁻] = (2s)²(s) = 4s³.
The most tested trap in all of equilibrium
Pure solids and pure liquids never appear in K expressions. Their concentration is effectively constant — it doesn't change as the reaction proceeds. CaCO₃(s) ⇌ CaO(s) + CO₂(g): both solids vanish. Kc = [CO₂] alone. This appears in at least one JAMB question every year.
Kc ↔ Kp conversion
Kp = Kc(RT)^Δn
R = 8.314 J/mol·K · T in Kelvin · Δn = moles gaseous products − moles gaseous reactants. If Δn = 0, then Kp = Kc. This is JAMB's favourite shortcut question.

Q is the reaction quotient — it uses the exact same formula as K, but with current concentrations, not equilibrium ones. The comparison of Q to K is how you predict which way a reaction will shift.

Q vs K — predicting the direction of shift

The p-notation is simply the negative logarithm of any quantity. It exists to compress very small numbers — Ka values like 1.8 × 10⁻⁵ — into manageable form. Once you see pX = −log[X], the entire pH/pOH/pKa/pKb system becomes the same operation applied to different quantities.

pH−log[H⁺]pH + pOH = 14 at 25°CpH < 7 = acidic. pH > 7 = basic. pH = 7 = neutral (at 25°C only).
pOH−log[OH⁻]pOH = 14 − pHFind [OH⁻] → get pOH → subtract from 14 to get pH. Three-step route JAMB tests.

The Act 2 principle
Every K expression is the same fraction, dressed differently for different arenas. Learn the rule once — products over reactants, powers from coefficients, solids excluded — and you can write any K expression JAMB will ever ask for.
Act Three

The World — Equilibrium Everywhere

Equilibrium is not a laboratory concept. It is the mechanism behind esterification, behind the body's pH regulation, behind how indicators change colour, behind why solids precipitate, behind how industrial ammonia is made. Every domain is just equilibrium wearing a different coat.

Organic Chemistry

Organic reactions hide equilibrium behind single arrows. Most organic reactions have K so large that the reverse is negligible — so chemists write → and move on. The exceptions are exactly what JAMB tests.

Conjugate acid-base pairs — Brønsted-Lowry in action
Every Brønsted-Lowry equilibrium produces a conjugate pair. In CH₃COOH + H₂O ⇌ CH₃COO⁻ + H₃O⁺: CH₃COOH and CH₃COO⁻ are a conjugate pair; H₂O and H₃O⁺ are a conjugate pair. In NH₃ + H₂O ⇌ NH₄⁺ + OH⁻: NH₃ and NH₄⁺ are a conjugate pair; H₂O and OH⁻ are a conjugate pair. The rule: conjugate base = acid minus one H⁺. Conjugate acid = base plus one H⁺. Water appears in both — it is amphoteric by the Brønsted-Lowry definition.

Iron catalyst speeds equilibrium without shifting it. Most tested industrial equilibrium. Know: conditions, reasons for each, why yield is only ~15% at 450°C, what Le Chatelier predicts vs what industry actually does.
Contact Process (SO₃) 2SO₂ + O₂ ⇌ 2SO₃ Exothermic forward. High pressure and low temperature favour SO₃. V₂O₅ catalyst. 450°C compromise again — same industrial logic as Haber. Second most tested industrial equilibrium. SO₃ + H₂O → H₂SO₄. Acid rain: unabsorbed SO₂ escapes → SO₂ + H₂O → H₂SO₃.

Neutral solution always has [H⁺] = [OH⁻], but this equals 10⁻⁷ only at 25°C. At 37°C, neutral pH ≈ 6.8. "Neutral" ≠ "pH 7" in general.

Common ion effect is Le Chatelier applied to Ksp. Adding a common ion reduces solubility. Tested in qualitative analysis and precipitation questions.
Carbonate decomposition CaCO₃(s) ⇌ CaO(s) + CO₂(g) Kc = [CO₂] only — both solids excluded. Increase pressure → Q > Kc → shifts back → less decomposition. Increase temperature → shifts forward (endothermic). Canonical example of solid exclusion from K expression. Appears in nearly every JAMB past paper.

Visible colour change demonstrates Le Chatelier directly. A favourite JAMB demonstration question. The colour change is the equilibrium shift — no calculation needed, just direction of shift with pH change.
Titration, Indicators & Buffers

The titration flask is an equilibrium arena. Every indicator is a weak acid equilibrium. Every buffer solution is Le Chatelier holding the line. These are not separate topics — they are the same system, applied to the lab bench.

Methyl orange changes too early — wrong indicator = wrong endpoint. Salt formed (CH₃COO⁻) hydrolyses: CH₃COO⁻ + H₂O ⇌ CH₃COOH + OH⁻. This is Kb of the conjugate base operating. Endpoint pH = 8–9.
Strong acid / Weak base HCl + NH₃ → NH₄Cl HNO₃ + NH₃ → NH₄NO₃ Methyl orange only. Endpoint pH < 7 (acidic salt hydrolysis). Phenolphthalein changes too late. NH₄⁺ hydrolyses: NH₄⁺ + H₂O ⇌ NH₃ + H₃O⁺. Ka of NH₄⁺ is active. Endpoint pH ≈ 5.
Buffer action CH₃COOH/CH₃COONa — resists pH drop when acid added NH₃/NH₄Cl — resists pH rise when base added Henderson-Hasselbalch: pH = pKa + log([A⁻]/[HA]). Half-equivalence point: [HA] = [A⁻] → pH = pKa exactly. JAMB tests this identity. Buffer IS the equilibrium. Add H⁺ → CH₃COO⁻ absorbs it (shifts left). Add OH⁻ → CH₃COOH releases H⁺ (shifts right). K unchanged throughout.
Real Life Processes
  • Blood pH regulation (CO₂/HCO₃⁻ buffer) — CO₂ + H₂O ⇌ H₂CO₃ ⇌ H⁺ + HCO₃⁻. Lungs control CO₂; kidneys control HCO₃⁻. Together they hold blood pH at 7.35–7.45. Deviate by 0.1 units — medical emergency. This is Le Chatelier keeping you alive.
  • Haemoglobin / oxygen binding — Hb + O₂ ⇌ HbO₂. In lungs (high O₂): shifts forward. In tissues (low O₂, high CO₂): shifts backward, releasing O₂. Equilibrium carries every molecule of oxygen in your body.
  • Tooth enamel dissolution — Ca₅(PO₄)₃OH(s) ⇌ 5Ca²⁺ + 3PO₄³⁻ + OH⁻. Ksp governs this. Acid in the mouth lowers [OH⁻] → Q < Ksp → enamel dissolves. Fluoride increases Ksp of the fluoride analogue — teeth resist acid. Chemistry in your mouth, every meal.
  • Ocean acidification — CO₂ + H₂O ⇌ H₂CO₃ ⇌ H⁺ + HCO₃⁻. Rising atmospheric CO₂ → more dissolves in ocean → equilibrium shifts forward → [H⁺] rises → pH drops. The same carbonic acid equilibrium that runs your blood is dissolving coral reefs.
  • Soap and hard water (Ksp) — Ca²⁺ + 2RCOO⁻ ⇌ Ca(RCOO)₂(s). Calcium ions in hard water precipitate soap scum. Ksp exceeded → precipitate forms. Softeners replace Ca²⁺ with Na⁺ (more soluble soap). Equilibrium solubility in the shower.
  • Fermentation (alcohol) — C₆H₁₂O₆ ⇌ 2C₂H₅OH + 2CO₂. Yeast enzymes catalyse — faster equilibrium, not different equilibrium. CO₂ removal (open vessel) prevents reverse. Product removal shifts forward. Industrial fermenters exploit Le Chatelier.
Why this maps directly to JAMB questions

Every cluster in this article corresponds to a category of JAMB question. Writing K expressions → tests solid/liquid exclusion and stoichiometric powers. Kc/Kp conversion → tests Δn and the special case Δn = 0. Le Chatelier → tests conditions for industrial processes (Haber, Contact). Q vs K → predicts shift direction. pH/pOH → three-step calculation questions. Ksp → solubility and common ion effect. Indicator choice → identifies titration type from endpoint pH. Buffer → Henderson-Hasselbalch at half-equivalence point. The thread connecting every question is always the same: products over reactants, the system resisting disturbance, and K as the fixed target the system always moves toward.

The closing compression
Equilibrium is chemistry's way of never fully committing. The tide always comes in and always goes out. Every reaction that matters — in industry, in the lab, in the body, in the ocean — is just a system finding the exact point where both directions are equally tired. Understand that one idea, and the symbols, laws, and principles all fall into place behind it.

From the beach where the tide holds steady to the blood vessel where pH never wavers — it was always the same question: where does the system want to rest?
