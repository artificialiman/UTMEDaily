🔢 Algebraic Fractions & LCM Methods

100 Questions · Addition, Subtraction, Equations, Simplification · LCM Strategy Throughout · BECE Standard

🔑 The LCM Rule for Fractions To add or subtract algebraic fractions, find the Lowest Common Multiple (LCM) of all denominators, rewrite each fraction with that LCM as the new denominator, then combine numerators. To solve an equation with fractions, multiply every term by the LCM to clear all denominators at once.

LCM of expressions: (i) numeric LCM × (ii) highest power of each variable × (iii) each distinct linear factor. Example: LCM of \(2x\) and \(3x^2\) is \(6x^2\). LCM of \((x-1)\) and \((x+2)\) is \((x-1)(x+2)\).

§C — Linear Factors in Denominator (Q21–Q36)
QUESTION 21
What is the LCM of \((x+1)\) and \((x-1)\)?
(A) \(x^2-1\)(B) \((x+1)(x-1)\)(C) \(x-1\)(D) Both A and B
Solution: They share no common factor, so LCM = \((x+1)(x-1) = x^2-1\). All of A, B and D are equivalent — the best answer to select is D.
Answer: D
QUESTION 22
Simplify \(\dfrac{1}{x+1} + \dfrac{1}{x-1}\).
