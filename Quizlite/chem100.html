⬡ Block 1: IUPAC Nomenclature — 7 Flow Questions
Q 01IUPAC
What is the IUPAC name of the compound with condensed structural formula CH₃CH₂CH₂CH₂CH₃?
(A) Butane(B) Pentane (C) Hexane(D) Propane
Solution: Count the carbon chain: C–C–C–C–C = 5 carbons. The prefix for 5 carbons is pent-; all single bonds means the suffix is -ane. Name = Pentane.
Answer: B — Pentane
Q 02IUPAC
Name the compound: CH₃CH(CH₃)CH₂CH₃
(A) 2-methylbutane(B) 3-methylbutane (C) 2-methylpentane(D) Isopentane
Solution: Longest chain through the branch = 4 carbons (butane backbone). Methyl group is on C-2. IUPAC = 2-methylbutane. "Isopentane" is a non-IUPAC trivial name and is never acceptable in IUPAC questions.
Answer: A — 2-methylbutane
Q 03IUPAC
Give the IUPAC name for: CH₂=CHCH₂CH₃
(A) 1-butene(B) 2-butene (C) But-1-yne(D) Butadiene
Solution: Four-carbon chain (but-), double bond starting at C-1 (-ene). The double bond is between C-1 and C-2, so locant = 1. IUPAC = But-1-ene (commonly written 1-butene). Option B is incorrect because the double bond is at C-1, not C-2.
Answer: A — 1-butene
Q 04IUPAC
The IUPAC name for CH₃COOH is:
(A) Methanoic acid(B) Ethanoic acid (C) Propanoic acid(D) Acetic acid
Solution: Total carbons including the carboxyl carbon = 2. Prefix: ethan-. Carboxylic acid suffix: -oic acid. Name = Ethanoic acid. "Acetic acid" is the non-IUPAC trivial name.
Answer: B — Ethanoic acid
Q 05IUPAC
What is the IUPAC name for C₂H₅OH?
(A) Methanol(B) Propan-1-ol (C) Ethan-1-ol(D) Ethyl alcohol
Solution: 2-carbon chain (ethan-) + alcohol group at C-1 (-1-ol). IUPAC = Ethan-1-ol. "Ethyl alcohol" is the trivial name; at JAMB level "Ethanol" is also accepted, but the full locant form is ethan-1-ol.
Answer: C — Ethan-1-ol
Q 06IUPAC
Identify the homologous series to which CH₃OCH₃ belongs and give its IUPAC name.
(A) Ketone — propanone(B) Ether — methoxymethane (C) Aldehyde — propanal(D) Alcohol — propan-2-ol
Solution: Two methyl groups flanking an oxygen: this is a symmetrical ether. IUPAC name = methoxymethane. Ethers are named alkoxy-alkane: smaller alkyl group becomes the alkoxy prefix (methoxy-), larger = parent (methane). Molecular formula C₂H₆O matches both dimethyl ether and ethanol — they are constitutional isomers.
Answer: B — Ether / methoxymethane
Q 07IUPAC
A compound has the condensed formula CH₃COCH₂CH₃. Name it by IUPAC rules.
(A) Butanone(B) Propanal (C) Butan-1-one(D) Pentan-2-one
Solution: 4-carbon chain with a C=O group not at the terminal position → ketone. C=O is at C-2 of a 4-carbon chain. IUPAC = Butan-2-one (commonly called butanone since C-2 is the only possible position for a ketone in butane). Option A "Butanone" is the shortened, accepted name. The numbering must give the carbonyl the lowest possible locant.
Answer: A — Butanone (Butan-2-one)
⬡ Block 2: Isomers & Structural Formulae — 7 Flow Questions
Q 08Isomers
How many structural (constitutional) isomers does butane (C₄H₁₀) have?
(A) 1(B) 2 (C) 3(D) 4
Solution: C₄H₁₀ has exactly 2 structural isomers: n-butane (CH₃CH₂CH₂CH₃) and 2-methylpropane / isobutane (CH₃CH(CH₃)CH₃). No more arrangements are possible.
Answer: B — 2
Q 09Isomers
Which pair of compounds are functional group isomers?
(A) Ethanol and dimethyl ether(B) n-butane and 2-methylpropane (C) Propan-1-ol and propan-2-ol(D) Ethanoic acid and methyl methanoate
Solution: Functional group isomers share the same molecular formula but belong to different functional group families. Both options A and D qualify, but option A (C₂H₆O: alcohol ↔ ether) and D (C₂H₄O₂: carboxylic acid ↔ ester) are functional group isomers. Option B is a chain isomer pair. Option C is a position isomer pair. Since A is the simpler, most commonly tested case at SS level, A is the standard answer; D is equally valid. Expect A in JAMB.
Answer: A — Ethanol and dimethyl ether
Q 10Isomers
Propan-1-ol and propan-2-ol are examples of which type of isomerism?
(A) Chain isomerism(B) Functional group isomerism (C) Position isomerism(D) Optical isomerism
Solution: Both have the formula C₃H₇OH with the same functional group (–OH) and the same carbon chain length (propyl), but the –OH group is on different carbons (C-1 vs C-2). This is position isomerism.
Answer: C — Position isomerism
Q 11Isomers
The molecular formula C₃H₆O could represent which two different functional group classes?
(A) Aldehyde and ketone(B) Alcohol and ether (C) Carboxylic acid and ester(D) Alkene and cycloalkane
Solution: C₃H₆O: propanal (CH₃CH₂CHO, an aldehyde) and propanone (CH₃COCH₃, a ketone) both have this formula. They are functional group isomers. Note: also allyl alcohol and cyclopropanol, but the aldehyde/ketone pair is the standard JAMB answer.
Answer: A — Aldehyde and ketone
Q 12Isomers
A student draws the following structural formula. What is its molecular formula?
H H H H | | | | H-C-C-C-C-H | | | | H H H H
(A) C₄H₈(B) C₄H₁₀ (C) C₄H₆(D) C₃H₈
Solution: 4 carbon atoms, each with the maximum hydrogen count for a saturated compound: C₄H₂(4)+2 = C₄H₁₀. All bonds are single (saturated), consistent with the drawn structure. Formula = C₄H₁₀.
Answer: B — C₄H₁₀
Q 13Isomers
Which of the following is the condensed structural formula of 2-methylpropan-1-ol?
(A) (CH₃)₂CHCH₂OH(B) CH₃CH₂CH₂OH (C) (CH₃)₃COH(D) CH₃CHOHCH₃
Solution: 2-methylpropan-1-ol: propan-1-ol backbone with a methyl branch at C-2, and –OH at C-1. Structure: (CH₃)₂CHCH₂OH. Option B = propan-1-ol (no branch). Option C = 2-methylpropan-2-ol (–OH at C-2, tertiary). Option D = propan-2-ol.
Answer: A — (CH₃)₂CHCH₂OH
Q 14Isomers
Ethanoic acid (CH₃COOH) and methyl methanoate (HCOOCH₃) are isomers. What type of isomerism is this?
(A) Position isomerism(B) Chain isomerism (C) Functional group isomerism(D) Optical isomerism
Solution: Both have molecular formula C₂H₄O₂ but belong to different functional group families — a carboxylic acid and an ester. This is functional group isomerism.
Answer: C — Functional group isomerism
⬡ Block 3: Chemical Tests for Organic Compounds — 7 Flow Questions
Q 15Tests
Which reagent is used to distinguish an aldehyde from a ketone?
(A) Bromine water(B) Fehling's solution (C) Litmus paper(D) Lime water
Solution: Fehling's solution (or Tollens' reagent) distinguishes aldehydes from ketones. Aldehydes are reducing agents and reduce Fehling's solution from blue to a brick-red precipitate of Cu₂O. Ketones do not react. Bromine water tests for unsaturation, not the aldehyde/ketone distinction.
Answer: B — Fehling's solution
Q 16Tests
A gas decolourises bromine water. The gas is most likely:
(A) Methane(B) Ethene (C) Carbon dioxide(D) Hydrogen chloride
Solution: Bromine water is decolourised by compounds with C=C double bonds via an addition reaction (electrophilic addition of Br₂ across the double bond). Ethene (CH₂=CH₂) is an alkene and undergoes this reaction, forming 1,2-dibromoethane. Methane (an alkane) does not react with bromine water under normal conditions.
Answer: B — Ethene
Q 17Tests
Tollens' reagent (ammoniacal silver nitrate) is added to an unknown compound. A shiny silver mirror forms on the test tube. This confirms the presence of:
(A) A ketone(B) An alkene (C) An aldehyde(D) A carboxylic acid
Solution: The silver mirror test: aldehydes reduce Ag⁺ ions in Tollens' reagent to silver metal (mirror). Ketones are not oxidised under these mild conditions and give no silver mirror. The half-equation: RCHO + 2[Ag(NH₃)₂]⁺ + 2OH⁻ → RCOO⁻ + 2Ag↓ + 4NH₃ + H₂O.
Answer: C — An aldehyde
Q 18Tests
Which test confirms the presence of an alcohol (–OH group)?
(A) Add sodium metal; observe effervescence(B) Add Benedict's solution; observe colour change (C) Add lime water; observe white precipitate(D) Expose to UV light with Cl₂
Solution: Alcohols react with sodium metal to produce hydrogen gas (effervescence): 2ROH + 2Na → 2RONa + H₂↑. This confirms the active –OH hydrogen. Benedict's solution tests for reducing sugars, not alcohols. The reaction with Na is more specific for –OH than for –COOH (which also reacts, but is identified by its acidity).
Answer: A — Sodium metal / effervescence of H₂
Q 19Tests
A student suspects a substance is a carboxylic acid. Which two tests together confirm this?
(A) Turns litmus red + reacts with Na₂CO₃ giving CO₂(B) Fehling's test positive + decolourises KMnO₄ (C) Bromine water decolourised + silver mirror formed(D) Burns with sooty flame + insoluble in water
Solution: A carboxylic acid: (i) turns litmus red (acidic pH), and (ii) reacts with Na₂CO₃ (or NaHCO₃) to produce CO₂ gas (effervescence), which turns lime water milky. The equation: 2RCOOH + Na₂CO₃ → 2RCOONa + H₂O + CO₂↑. Phenols also turn litmus red but do not react with Na₂CO₃ to give CO₂ — this distinction is key in JAMB.
Answer: A — Litmus red + CO₂ from Na₂CO₃
Q 20Tests
The iodoform test (warm iodine in NaOH) gives a pale-yellow precipitate of CHI₃. This indicates the compound contains:
(A) A –CH₂OH group (primary alcohol with no methyl adjacent) (B) A CH₃CO– group (methyl ketone or ethanal / secondary alcohol giving CH₃CO–) (C) A –COOH group (D) A C=C double bond
Solution: The iodoform (triiodomethane) test is positive for compounds containing the CH₃CO– grouping or that can be oxidised to it. This includes: ethanal (CH₃CHO), methyl ketones (CH₃COR), ethanol (CH₃CH₂OH → oxidised to CH₃CHO), and secondary alcohols of the type CH₃CH(OH)R. The yellow precipitate is CHI₃ (iodoform). Propanone (CH₃COCH₃) is the classic positive test compound.
Answer: B — CH₃CO– grouping
Q 21Tests
A colourless gas burns with a blue flame and produces only CO₂ and H₂O. When lime water is added to the combustion products, it turns milky. The gas is most likely:
(A) Hydrogen(B) Methane (C) Carbon monoxide(D) Sulphur dioxide
Solution: The products CO₂ + H₂O confirm combustion of a hydrocarbon. CO burns to give only CO₂ (no water). Hydrogen burns to give only water. SO₂ is not produced by simple combustion here. Among the options, methane (CH₄) fits: CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O. Burns cleanly with a blue, non-sooty flame.
Answer: B — Methane
⬡ Block 4: Organic Reactions, By-products & Industry — 7 Flow Questions
Q 22Reactions
Ethanol reacts with ethanoic acid in the presence of a concentrated H₂SO₄ catalyst. What type of reaction is this, and what is the organic product?
(A) Addition; diethyl ether(B) Esterification; ethyl ethanoate (C) Oxidation; ethanal(D) Fermentation; ethanoic acid
Solution: This is an esterification reaction (acid + alcohol → ester + water). Concentrated H₂SO₄ acts as both catalyst and dehydrating agent. Product: ethyl ethanoate (CH₃COOC₂H₅) + H₂O. Equation: CH₃COOH + C₂H₅OH ⇌ CH₃COOC₂H₅ + H₂O. The reaction is reversible; the ester is removed to shift equilibrium right.
Answer: B — Esterification; ethyl ethanoate
Q 23Reactions
In the industrial manufacture of ethanol by fermentation of glucose, what are the exact products?
(A) Ethanol + oxygen(B) Ethanol + carbon dioxide (C) Ethanol + water(D) Ethanol + methane
Solution: Fermentation equation: C₆H₁₂O₆ → 2C₂H₅OH + 2CO₂ (catalysed by yeast enzymes / zymase, at ~35°C, in absence of oxygen). The by-product is carbon dioxide. This is an anaerobic process; oxygen stops fermentation.
Answer: B — Ethanol + carbon dioxide
Q 24Reactions
Cracking of long-chain alkanes produces shorter-chain alkanes and:
(A) Alkynes only(B) Alkenes (C) Alcohols(D) Noble gases
Solution: Thermal cracking of long-chain hydrocarbons (e.g., C₁₆H₃₄) breaks C–C bonds to produce shorter alkanes and alkenes. Example: C₁₀H₂₂ → C₅H₁₂ + C₅H₁₀. The alkene (high octane / monomer for polymers like polyethene) is the commercially valuable by-product of cracking. Alkenes are not present in the original crude oil fraction.
Answer: B — Alkenes
Q 25Reactions
When ethene undergoes addition polymerisation, the product is:
(A) Polypropene(B) Polyvinyl chloride (PVC) (C) Polyethene (polythene)(D) Nylon
Solution: Addition polymerisation of ethene (CH₂=CH₂) produces polyethene: nCH₂=CH₂ → –(CH₂CH₂)ₙ–. No other molecule is expelled; this is addition (not condensation) polymerisation. PVC uses vinyl chloride (CH₂=CHCl) as monomer. Nylon is a condensation polymer.
Answer: C — Polyethene
Q 26Reactions
Saponification is the process of:
(A) Esterification of fatty acids with glycerol (B) Hydrolysis of an ester by a strong alkali to form soap (salt of fatty acid) and glycerol (C) Oxidation of alkenes to aldehydes (D) Dehydration of alcohols to form alkenes
Solution: Saponification = alkaline hydrolysis of a fat (triester of glycerol + fatty acids) using concentrated NaOH or KOH: fat + 3NaOH → glycerol + 3 sodium salt (soap). The word comes from Latin sapo (soap). This is the industrial basis of soap-making. Option A describes the reverse process (esterification to form a fat).
Answer: B — Alkaline hydrolysis of fat to form soap
Q 27By-products
In the industrial production of soap, what is the by-product of saponification that is commercially valuable?
(A) Ethanol(B) Glycerol (propane-1,2,3-triol) (C) Ethene(D) Methanol
Solution: The by-product is glycerol (HOCH₂CHOHCH₂OH). Glycerol is commercially used in cosmetics, pharmaceuticals, food processing, and as a precursor to nitroglycerin. The fat/oil itself is the triester; NaOH breaks all three ester bonds releasing glycerol and three carboxylate (soap) molecules.
Answer: B — Glycerol
Q 28Reactions
The dehydration of ethanol at 170°C with excess concentrated H₂SO₄ produces:
(A) Ethane(B) Diethyl ether (C) Ethene(D) Ethanal
Solution: At 170°C (excess H₂SO₄), intramolecular dehydration of one ethanol molecule eliminates H₂O to form ethene: C₂H₅OH → CH₂=CH₂ + H₂O. At 140°C (equimolar ethanol), intermolecular dehydration of two ethanol molecules gives diethyl ether. Temperature controls the product — a key JAMB distinction.
Answer: C — Ethene (at 170°C)
⚙ Block 5: Industrial Chemistry — 7 Flow Questions
Q 29Industrial
In the Haber process for ammonia synthesis, what are the optimum industrial conditions?
(A) 200°C, 1 atm, platinum catalyst (B) 450°C, 200 atm, iron catalyst (C) 500°C, 1000 atm, vanadium pentoxide catalyst (D) 25°C, 500 atm, nickel catalyst
Solution: Haber process: N₂ + 3H₂ ⇌ 2NH₃ (ΔH = −92 kJ/mol). Industrial conditions: temperature ≈ 450°C, pressure ≈ 150–300 atm (typically cited as 200 atm), iron catalyst (with K₂O promoter). Higher pressure favours product (fewer moles of gas), but lower temperature is needed for feasibility and economics — 450°C is a compromise. Option C (V₂O₅) is the Contact process (H₂SO₄), not Haber.
Answer: B — 450°C, 200 atm, iron catalyst
Q 30Industrial
The Contact process is used to manufacture which acid?
(A) Hydrochloric acid(B) Nitric acid (C) Sulphuric acid(D) Phosphoric acid
Solution: The Contact process manufactures sulphuric acid (H₂SO₄) in three steps: (1) S + O₂ → SO₂; (2) 2SO₂ + O₂ ⇌ 2SO₃ (450°C, V₂O₅); (3) SO₃ is absorbed in 98% H₂SO₄ (oleum) then diluted. Nitric acid is made by the Ostwald process. HCl is made by burning H₂ in Cl₂ or as a by-product of chlorination.
Answer: C — Sulphuric acid
Q 31Industrial
In the Contact process, why is SO₃ not directly dissolved in water to form H₂SO₄?
(A) The reaction is too slow (B) It produces a corrosive acid mist that is difficult to handle and absorb (C) Water is not available industrially (D) SO₃ is insoluble in water
Solution: When SO₃ is mixed with water, it forms a fine acid mist (H₂SO₄ aerosol) that is extremely difficult to contain and absorb safely in industrial scrubbers. Instead, SO₃ is absorbed into 98% concentrated H₂SO₄ to form oleum (H₂S₂O₇), which is then carefully diluted with measured water to give the required concentration of H₂SO₄.
Answer: B — Produces acid mist
Q 32Industrial
Which raw material is the primary source of nitrogen for the Haber process?
(A) Liquid nitrogen from cryogenic plants(B) Nitrogen from air (fractional distillation of liquid air) (C) Nitrogen from ammonia decomposition(D) Nitrogen from sodium nitrate
Solution: Nitrogen (78% of air by volume) is obtained cheaply by fractional distillation of liquid air. The source of hydrogen is natural gas (methane) via steam reforming: CH₄ + H₂O → CO + 3H₂, followed by the water-gas shift reaction. Air → liquid air (cooling) → fractional distillation → N₂ (bp −196°C) separated from O₂ (bp −183°C).
Answer: B — Fractional distillation of air
Q 33Industrial
In the Blast Furnace, the primary reducing agent that reduces iron ore is:
(A) Carbon (coke)(B) Carbon monoxide (C) Hydrogen(D) Calcium oxide
Solution: Carbon monoxide is the direct reducing agent: Fe₂O₃ + 3CO → 2Fe + 3CO₂. CO is formed from coke burning in a limited air supply at the base: 2C + O₂ → 2CO. So coke generates CO, which then reduces the ore. The question specifically asks for the primary reducing agent, which is CO, not coke directly. CaO is a flux that removes SiO₂ impurities as slag (CaSiO₃).
Answer: B — Carbon monoxide
Q 34Industrial
The Solvay process produces which industrial chemical?
(A) Sodium hydroxide from brine(B) Sodium carbonate (washing soda) from brine and limestone (C) Chlorine gas from molten NaCl(D) Ammonia from nitrogen and hydrogen
Solution: The Solvay (ammonia-soda) process produces sodium carbonate (Na₂CO₃) from: saturated NaCl (brine) + CO₂ + NH₃ → NaHCO₃ precipitate → heated → Na₂CO₃ + CO₂ + H₂O. The NH₃ is recycled. Na₂CO₃ is used in glass-making, paper, detergents, and as a water softener.
Answer: B — Sodium carbonate
Q 35Industrial
In petroleum refining, which fraction is collected at the lowest temperature in fractional distillation?
(A) Fuel oil / bitumen(B) Kerosene (C) Petroleum gases (LPG)(D) Diesel
Solution: In the fractionating column, temperature decreases from bottom (highest boiling fractions) to top (lowest boiling fractions). Petroleum gases (C₁–C₄ hydrocarbons: methane, ethane, propane, butane) have the lowest boiling points and exit at the top. Bitumen/residue exits at the base (~350°C+). Order from top (coldest): gases → petrol → naphtha → kerosene → diesel → fuel oil → bitumen.
Answer: C — Petroleum gases (LPG)
⚙ Block 6: Electrolysis — Molten vs Aqueous, Anode/Cathode, Cell Conversions — 7 Flow Questions
Q 36Electrolysis
During electrolysis, which statement about electrodes is always correct?
(A) Reduction occurs at the anode (B) Oxidation occurs at the cathode (C) Cations migrate to and are discharged at the cathode (reduction) (D) Anions are discharged at the cathode
Solution: The mnemonic: OILRIG at electrodes — Oxidation at anode, Reduction at cathode. Cations (positive ions) migrate to the cathode (negative electrode) where they gain electrons (reduction). Anions (negative ions) migrate to the anode (positive electrode) where they lose electrons (oxidation). Option C is the correct statement.
Answer: C — Cations reduced at cathode
Q 37Electrolysis
When molten sodium chloride (NaCl) is electrolysed, what is produced at each electrode?
(A) Cathode: Na metal; Anode: Cl₂ gas (B) Cathode: Cl₂ gas; Anode: Na metal (C) Cathode: H₂ gas; Anode: Cl₂ gas (D) Cathode: Na metal; Anode: O₂ gas
Solution: In molten NaCl, only Na⁺ and Cl⁻ ions are present (no water). Cathode: Na⁺ + e⁻ → Na (sodium metal). Anode: 2Cl⁻ → Cl₂ + 2e⁻ (chlorine gas). Key distinction: in aqueous NaCl, H₂ is produced at the cathode (H⁺ from water is preferentially discharged over Na⁺ because Na is far above hydrogen in the reactivity series).
Answer: A — Na at cathode; Cl₂ at anode
Q 38Electrolysis
When concentrated aqueous NaCl (brine) is electrolysed using inert electrodes, what is produced at the cathode?
(A) Sodium metal(B) Chlorine gas (C) Hydrogen gas(D) Oxygen gas
Solution: In aqueous NaCl, both Na⁺ and H⁺ (from water: H₂O ⇌ H⁺ + OH⁻) are present at the cathode. H⁺ is preferentially discharged because Na is very high in the electrochemical series (very difficult to reduce). Cathode: 2H⁺ + 2e⁻ → H₂↑. Anode: 2Cl⁻ → Cl₂ + 2e⁻ (Cl⁻ discharged over OH⁻ in concentrated brine). The solution becomes alkaline (NaOH remains).
Answer: C — Hydrogen gas
Q 39Electrolysis
In the electrolysis of dilute sulphuric acid (H₂SO₄) with inert (platinum) electrodes, what gas is produced at the anode?
(A) Hydrogen(B) Sulphur dioxide (C) Oxygen(D) Sulphur trioxide
Solution: In dilute H₂SO₄, the anode ions present are SO₄²⁻ and OH⁻ (from water). OH⁻ is preferentially discharged over SO₄²⁻ (sulphate requires far more energy to oxidise): 4OH⁻ → O₂ + 2H₂O + 4e⁻. Gas at anode = oxygen. Cathode: 2H⁺ + 2e⁻ → H₂. Volume ratio H₂:O₂ = 2:1 (Hofmann voltameter). SO₄²⁻ is not discharged in dilute acid with inert electrodes.
Answer: C — Oxygen
Q 40Electrolysis
In the electrolytic refining of copper, which of the following correctly describes what happens?
(A) Cathode = pure Cu anode; Anode = impure Cu cathode (B) Anode = impure Cu; Cathode = pure Cu; electrolyte = CuSO₄ solution (C) Both electrodes are inert; Cu²⁺ deposited from solution (D) Anode = pure Cu; Cathode = impure Cu; electrolyte = NaCl
Solution: Electrolytic refining of copper: Anode = impure (blister) copper (it dissolves: Cu → Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻). Cathode = thin sheet of pure copper (Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu deposited). Electrolyte = CuSO₄ solution. Impurities (Ag, Au, Pt) fall as anode sludge and are commercially valuable. The electrolyte concentration stays approximately constant as Cu dissolves at the same rate it deposits.
Answer: B — Impure Cu anode; Pure Cu cathode; CuSO₄
Q 41Electrolysis
By Faraday's first law of electrolysis, if 2 moles of electrons pass through a CuSO₄ solution, how many grams of copper are deposited? (Relative atomic mass of Cu = 64; Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu)
(A) 32 g(B) 64 g (C) 128 g(D) 16 g
Solution: Each Cu²⁺ ion requires 2 electrons. 2 moles of electrons deposit 1 mole of Cu = 64 g. Using Faraday's law: mass = (Q / F) × (M / n) where Q = 2F, n = 2 (charge on Cu²⁺), M = 64. Mass = (2F/F) × (64/2) = 2 × 32 = 64 g. Note: 1 Faraday (F) = 96,500 C = charge of 1 mole of electrons.
Answer: B — 64 g
Q 42Electrolysis
In the electrolysis of aluminium oxide (Al₂O₃) dissolved in molten cryolite, why is cryolite used?
(A) To increase the purity of aluminium (B) To lower the melting point of Al₂O₃ from ~2050°C to ~950°C, reducing energy costs (C) To act as the reducing agent for Al³⁺ (D) To prevent the reaction between Al and O₂
Solution: Al₂O₃ melts at ~2050°C — far too high for industrial operation (electrode degradation, enormous energy costs). Cryolite (Na₃AlF₆) dissolves Al₂O₃ and reduces the operating temperature to ~950°C. This makes the Hall-Héroult process economically viable. Cathode: Al³⁺ + 3e⁻ → Al (liquid). Anode: 2O²⁻ → O₂ + 4e⁻ (carbon anodes oxidised/burned by O₂, requiring regular replacement).
Answer: B — Lowers melting point to ~950°C
⚙ Block 7: Solubility Calculations — Anhydrous ↔ Hydrated, Conversions — 7 Flow Questions
Q 43Solubility
The solubility of a substance is defined as:
(A) The mass of solute that dissolves in 1 000 g of water at a given temperature (B) The mass of solute in grams that dissolves in 100 g of solvent to form a saturated solution at a stated temperature (C) The number of moles of solute per litre of solution (D) The percentage by mass of solute in the solution
Solution: Solubility is defined as the mass of solute (in grams) that dissolves in 100 g of solvent to form a saturated solution at a stated temperature. Option C defines molarity (mol/dm³); Option D defines percentage composition. The "100 g of solvent" definition is standard at SS/JAMB level.
Answer: B — g per 100 g solvent at given temperature
Q 44Solubility
The solubility of potassium nitrate (KNO₃) at 60°C is 110 g per 100 g water. At 20°C it is 32 g per 100 g water. If 200 g of water at 60°C is saturated with KNO₃ and then cooled to 20°C, what mass of KNO₃ crystallises out?
(A) 78 g(B) 156 g (C) 110 g(D) 64 g
Solution: At 60°C: 200 g water dissolves 2 × 110 = 220 g KNO₃. At 20°C: 200 g water dissolves 2 × 32 = 64 g KNO₃. Mass crystallised = 220 − 64 = 156 g. Strategy: always scale solubility by the ratio (actual water / 100 g) before calculating dissolved mass at each temperature, then subtract.
Answer: B — 156 g
Q 45Solubility
Copper(II) sulphate crystals are CuSO₄·5H₂O (M = 250 g/mol). The anhydrous salt is CuSO₄ (M = 160 g/mol). If the solubility of CuSO₄·5H₂O is stated as 40 g per 100 g water, what is the equivalent solubility expressed in terms of the anhydrous CuSO₄?

Q 86Reactions
Markovnikov's rule predicts that in the addition of HBr to propene (CH₃CH=CH₂), the bromine atom adds to:
(A) The carbon with more hydrogen atoms (C-1) (B) The carbon with fewer hydrogen atoms (C-2, the more substituted carbon) (C) Either carbon — no preference (D) The double bond oxygen

Q 93Industrial
In which direction does the Haber process equilibrium shift if pressure is increased, and why?

Solution: N₂ + 3H₂ ⇌ 2NH₃. Left: 1 + 3 = 4 mol gas. Right: 2 mol gas. Higher pressure favours the side with fewer moles of gas (Le Chatelier's principle) → equilibrium shifts right (towards NH₃). Q 99Application
Suggest why the greenhouse effect itself is necessary for life on Earth, yet is currently a major environmental problem.

A chemist wishes to convert ethanoic acid (CH₃COOH) into ethanol (CH₃CH₂OH). Which sequence of reactions achieves this, and what reagents are needed?
(A) Add water and heat — esterification produces ethanol (B) Reduce with LiAlH₄ (or H₂/catalyst) — carboxylic acid → primary alcohol (reduction) (C) Add NaOH — neutralisation gives ethanol (D) Oxidise with K₂Cr₂O₇ — acid → alcohol
