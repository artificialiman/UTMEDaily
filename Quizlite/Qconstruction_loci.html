⊙ Block 1 — Compass & Ruler Basics · Q1–Q5
Q 01Tools · Flow
In a geometric construction, only two instruments are permitted. They are:
(A) Ruler and protractor (B) Compass and straightedge (ruler used only for drawing lines, not measuring) (C) Compass and protractor (D) Set square and ruler
Classic Euclidean construction tools: Compass → transfers lengths; draws arcs and circles Straightedge → draws straight lines through two known points (No measurements are read off the ruler — that is why it is called a "straightedge") A protractor reads angles off an existing figure — it does not construct them. Using a protractor or set square to set a 60° angle is measuring, not constructing. The compass-and-straightedge restriction is the defining constraint of the subject.
Answer: B — compass and straightedge
Q 02Copy a Line Segment
To copy a line segment PQ onto a ray starting from point A, the correct first step is:
(A) Draw a circle of radius PQ centred at P (B) Open the compass to the length PQ and draw an arc centred at A (C) Measure PQ with a ruler and mark the same length from A (D) Draw a perpendicular from P and transfer it to A
Copying a segment — the core compass transfer: Step 1: Place compass tip at P, pencil at Q — compass now holds length PQ. Step 2: Lift compass; place tip at A on the new ray. Step 3: Draw an arc — where it crosses the ray is point B, with AB = PQ. Key idea: the compass stores a length by keeping the same opening. Option C (using a ruler to measure) is not a construction — it is permitted in technical drawing but not in pure geometric construction. Option A draws a circle around P, which does not help place the length at A.
Answer: B — open compass to PQ, arc from A
Q 03Constructing a Triangle
To construct triangle ABC with AB = 6 cm, BC = 5 cm, and CA = 4 cm, which of the following steps is taken after drawing the base AB = 6 cm?
(A) Draw a 60° angle at A and mark off 5 cm (B) Draw an arc of radius 5 cm centred at B and an arc of radius 4 cm centred at A; their intersection is C (C) Bisect AB to find the midpoint and draw a perpendicular (D) Use a protractor to measure the angle at A from the given sides
SSS (Side-Side-Side) construction: Base AB = 6 cm is drawn first. Arc 1: centre B, radius 5 cm (all points 5 cm from B) Arc 2: centre A, radius 4 cm (all points 4 cm from A) Intersection of arcs → point C (the unique point satisfying both distances) Why it works: C must lie on both circles simultaneously — their intersection gives the only point that is exactly 5 cm from B AND 4 cm from A. Two circles of different radii in general position intersect at exactly two points; we pick the one above AB by convention.
Answer: B — arcs of radius 5 cm and 4 cm
Q 04Perpendicular from a Point on a Line
To erect a perpendicular to line l at a point P on the line, the standard compass procedure begins by:
(A) Drawing a large arc centred at P to find a distant point (B) Opening the compass to any convenient radius and striking two arcs on line l on both sides of P to find equidistant points A and B (C) Drawing a circle centred at P and finding its highest point (D) Placing the set square at P and tracing the 90° edge
Perpendicular at a point ON a line — full steps: Step 1: Compass at P, any radius r → mark A and B on line l (PA = PB = r). Step 2: Increase radius; draw arcs above l centred at A and at B. Step 3: These arcs intersect at Q above l. Step 4: Line PQ is perpendicular to l at P. Why it works: A and B are equidistant from P (both at radius r). Any point equidistant from A and B lies on the perpendicular bisector of AB — and P is the midpoint of AB, so PQ is exactly that perpendicular bisector, which meets l at a right angle.
Answer: B — equidistant points A and B on line l
Q 05Perpendicular from a Point OFF a Line
To drop a perpendicular from point P (not on line l) to line l, the first step is:
(A) Draw a line from P parallel to l (B) Draw an arc centred at any point on l passing through P (C) Draw an arc centred at P (large enough radius) to cut line l at two points A and B (D) Mark the foot of the perpendicular by measuring the shortest distance
Perpendicular from P to line l — full steps: Step 1: Compass at P, radius large enough → cut l at A and at B (PA = PB). Step 2: From A and from B, draw arcs of equal radius on the far side of l. Step 3: These arcs intersect at Q (on the opposite side of l from P). Step 4: Line PQ meets l perpendicularly at foot F (midpoint of AB). Core distinction: perpendicular AT a point on the line (Q4) vs. perpendicular FROM a point off the line (Q5) — both start with the compass at P or on the line, but the geometry is slightly different. In Q4 we cut l on both sides of P; in Q5 we cut l from P's arc above it.
Answer: C — arc from P cutting l at A and B
∠ Block 2 — Angle Constructions · Q6–Q10
Q 06Constructing 60°
The standard construction for a 60° angle uses the fact that:
(A) A diameter subtends a 90° angle at the circumference (B) The perpendicular bisector of a segment makes two 90° angles (C) An equilateral triangle has all angles equal to 60°, and is constructed by two arcs of the same radius (D) A 90° angle bisected gives 45°, and halved again gives 60°
60° construction — equilateral triangle method: Step 1: Draw ray from O through A (base of angle). Step 2: Compass at O, radius OA → arc above the line, cutting at point on arc. Step 3: Same radius, compass at A → arc cutting the first arc at B. Now OA = OB = AB → triangle OAB is equilateral. Step 4: Angle AOB = 60°. Key insight: three arcs of equal radius form an equilateral triangle — all sides equal means all angles equal 60°. This is the most fundamental angle construction; 30°, 120°, and 150° are all derived from it. Option D (halving 90° to get 45°, then 60°) is arithmetically wrong — 90°/2 = 45°, not 60°.
Answer: C — equilateral triangle, same radius arcs
Q 07Constructing 90°
To construct a 90° angle at point O on a line, the method that does NOT rely on a perpendicular bisector is:
(A) Bisect a straight angle (180°) (B) Bisect two adjacent 45° angles (C) Construct a 60° angle, then bisect the remaining 120° to get 60°, giving 60° + 30° = 90° (D) Draw a semicircle and find the point on its diameter
Standard method — bisecting the straight angle: Easiest construction of 90° at O: A straight angle (flat line through O) = 180°. Bisect it with the perpendicular bisector construction → 90° on each side. Alternative — via 60°: Construct 60°. The remaining angle to complete 180° is 120°. Bisect 120° → two 60° angles. 60° + 30° = 90°. ✓ (The "60° + its bisected supplement" method) Option A describes the most common route. Option C is a valid alternative derivation. The question asks for the method that does NOT use a perpendicular bisector — that points to C, which builds 90° from 60° via bisection of 120°.
Answer: C — 60° + bisected 120° = 90°
Q 08Constructing 30° and 45°
Match each angle with its correct construction route.
(A) 30° = bisect 90°;   45° = bisect 60° (B) 30° = bisect 60°;   45° = bisect 90° (C) 30° = bisect 90°;   45° = bisect 90° + bisect 90° (D) 30° = construct from scratch;   45° = construct from scratch
Derived angles — always halve the parent: 60° ÷ 2 = 30° → bisect a constructed 60° angle 90° ÷ 2 = 45° → bisect a constructed 90° angle General principle: angle bisection is the fundamental halving tool. Any constructible angle can be halved infinitely. Option A reverses the parent angles (30° is not half of 90° = 45°). The hierarchy is: 180° (straight) → bisect → 90° → bisect → 45° → bisect → 22.5° 60° (equilateral) → bisect → 30° → bisect → 15° 90° + 60° = 150°; 90° − 60° = 30°; 60° + 30° = 90° (cross-check)
Answer: B — 30° = bisect 60°; 45° = bisect 90°
Q 09Constructing 120° and 150°
A student needs to construct a 150° angle. Which sequence of constructions achieves this?
(A) Bisect 180° to get 90°, then bisect 90° to get 45°, then add 45° + 105° (B) Construct 60°, then construct another 60° adjacent, giving 120°, then bisect the remaining 60° to add 30° (C) Construct 60° at O. The supplement of 60° on a straight line is 120°. Bisect 60° to get 30°. Then 120° + 30° = 150°. (D) Bisect 90° twice to get 22.5° and add multiples
150° = 90° + 60°, or equivalently 180° − 30°. Method (Option C): 1. Construct 60° at O on ray OA. 2. Remaining supplement = 180° − 60° = 120° (on the other side of OB from 60°). 3. Bisect 60° → get 30°. 4. 120° + 30° = 150° ✓ Alternative — even simpler: 1. Construct 180° (straight line through O). 2. Construct 30° from one end: bisect 60° as above. 3. 180° − 30° = 150° from the other side. Option B gives 120° correctly (two adjacent 60° angles) but then adds 30° incorrectly — bisecting the remaining 60° gives 30° to add, making 120° + 30° = 150° — so it actually works too, but option C is the cleaner and more standard route taught in WAEC papers.
Answer: C — 180° − 30°, using 60° bisected
Q 10Copying an Angle
To copy angle BAC onto a ray DE, the essential idea is to:
(A) Measure the angle with a protractor and redraw it at D (B) Bisect angle BAC and transfer the bisector (C) Use arcs to transfer two equal triangles: the arc-and-chord that defines angle BAC is reproduced at D with the same arc radius and chord length (D) Extend the sides of angle BAC to reach DE
Copying an angle — full procedure: Step 1: Draw arc centred at A (radius r) → cuts AB at P and AC at Q. Step 2: Draw same arc (radius r) centred at D → cuts DE at P'. Step 3: Set compass to chord PQ; place at P' → cut arc at Q'. Step 4: Ray DQ' makes the same angle with DE as angle BAC. Why: The two arcs are congruent (same radius); chords PQ = P'Q' → triangles APQ ≅ DP'Q' (SSS) → angles are equal. The arc-then-chord transfer is the standard WAEC construction answer. Option A (protractor) is measuring, not constructing. Option B (bisect and transfer) only works if you know both halves are equal — you'd still need to copy, not just bisect.
Answer: C — arc and chord transfer
⊥ Block 3 — Bisectors · Q11–Q15
Q 11Angle Bisector Construction
To bisect angle AOB, the correct procedure after drawing an arc centred at O (cutting OA at P and OB at Q) is:
(A) Draw a line from O through the midpoint of chord PQ (B) From P and Q, draw equal arcs that intersect at R; then OR bisects angle AOB (C) Measure the angle with a protractor and draw the half-angle (D) Draw a line from P perpendicular to OB
Angle bisector construction — full steps: Step 1: Compass at O, any radius → cuts OA at P, cuts OB at Q. Step 2: Compass at P, radius > ½PQ → arc inside the angle. Step 3: Same radius, compass at Q → arc intersecting the arc from P at R. Step 4: Ray OR is the angle bisector of angle AOB. Why it works: R is equidistant from the two arcs (OR = OP = OQ via the construction), so OR bisects the arc PQ. Since OP = OQ (same compass setting at O) and PR = QR (same compass setting at P and Q), triangles OPR ≅ OQR (SSS), so angles POR = QOR.
Option A (midpoint of chord PQ) gives a line bisecting the chord but not necessarily bisecting the angle, unless the triangle OPQ is isosceles — which it always is here, so both A and B are equivalent! However, B is the accepted standard procedure in WAEC marking schemes.
Answer: B — equal arcs from P and Q intersect at R; draw OR
Q 12Perpendicular Bisector
The perpendicular bisector of segment AB is the set of all points that are:
(A) On the same side of AB as a given point C (B) Equidistant from both endpoints A and B (C) At a fixed distance from the midpoint M of AB (D) On the circle with AB as diameter
Definition of perpendicular bisector as a locus: Perpendicular bisector of AB = {P : PA = PB} It passes through the midpoint M of AB and makes a 90° angle with AB. It is both: (i) a bisector — it passes through M, the midpoint (ii) perpendicular — it meets AB at 90° Connection to loci (Block 4): This is the locus of points equidistant from two fixed points — a straight line. Option C describes a circle (constant distance from a point), not the bisector. Option D describes the Thales circle (locus of points from which AB subtends 90°), which is different.
Answer: B — equidistant from A and B
Q 13Perpendicular Bisector Construction
To construct the perpendicular bisector of AB (length 8 cm), you open the compass to a radius greater than 4 cm. Why must the radius exceed 4 cm (i.e., half of AB)?
(A) So that the arcs are drawn on only one side of AB (B) So that the circles centred at A and B are congruent (C) So that the arcs from A and from B actually intersect — if the radius were less than 4 cm the circles would not reach each other (D) So that the bisector is long enough to measure
Intersection condition for two circles: Two circles of equal radius r, centres A and B separated by distance d = AB: Intersect in two points iff r > d/2 Touch at one point iff r = d/2 (degenerate — bisector would be a single point) Do not intersect iff r < d/2 Here d = AB = 8 cm, so r > 4 cm guarantees two intersection points above and below AB, giving the full perpendicular bisector. Standard WAEC instruction: "open the compass to more than half the length of the line." This question explains why that rule exists. Option B (congruent circles) is always true regardless of radius and does not address intersection.
Answer: C — arcs must reach each other to intersect
Q 14Locus Connection — Angle Bisector
The angle bisector of angle AOB is also the locus of all points that are:
(A) At the same distance from O as from A (B) Equidistant from the two arms OA and OB (i.e., same perpendicular distance to each line) (C) At a fixed distance from the vertex O (D) On the circle passing through O, A, and B
Angle bisector as a locus: For any point P on the bisector of angle AOB: (perpendicular distance from P to line OA) = (perpendicular distance from P to line OB) This is the defining property of the angle bisector as a locus — it is the locus of points equidistant from two intersecting straight lines. Contrast with perpendicular bisector (Q12): perpendicular bisector = equidistant from two points; angle bisector = equidistant from two lines. These two loci are the most commonly confused pair in the BECE/WAEC topic.
Answer: B — equidistant from the two arms (lines)
Q 15Circumcentre
The circumcentre of a triangle is found by:
(A) Constructing the three angle bisectors — they meet at the circumcentre (B) Constructing the perpendicular bisectors of the three sides — they meet at the circumcentre (C) Constructing the three medians — they meet at the circumcentre (D) Constructing the three altitudes — they meet at the circumcentre
Triangle centres — keep these distinct: Circumcentre (O) — intersection of perpendicular bisectors of the sides — equidistant from all 3 vertices; centre of circumscribed circle Incentre (I) — intersection of angle bisectors — equidistant from all 3 sides; centre of inscribed circle Centroid (G) — intersection of medians — centre of mass; divides each median 2:1 from vertex Orthocentre (H) — intersection of altitudes — no simple equal-distance property Memory hook: Circum(scribed) → Perpendicular bisectors (both deal with "outside" the triangle). In(scribed) → angle bisectors (both deal with "inside"). Option A describes the incentre, not the circumcentre.
Answer: B — perpendicular bisectors → circumcentre
◎ Block 4 — Loci: Definitions & Shapes · Q16–Q22
Q 16Definition of a Locus
A locus (plural: loci) in plane geometry is defined as:
(A) The set of all points that satisfy a given algebraic equation (B) The path traced by a moving point that maintains a fixed relationship with other geometric objects (C) Both A and B — the set of all points satisfying a given condition (geometric or algebraic) (D) The area enclosed by a curve
Locus — unified definition: A locus is the complete set of all points — and only those points — that satisfy a given condition or rule. The condition may be stated geometrically: "all points 3 cm from P" or algebraically: "all (x, y) with x² + y² = 9" Both describe the same circle of radius 3 centred at P. The word "locus" means "place" in Latin. Option D (area enclosed) confuses a locus (a boundary/line) with a region. The locus is the circle itself (the 1D curve), not the disk it encloses.
Answer: C — both geometric and algebraic: set of all points satisfying a condition
Q 17Locus at Fixed Distance from a Point
The locus of all points at a fixed distance r from a fixed point O is:
(A) A straight line (B) A circle of radius r centred at O (C) A parabola (D) Two parallel lines
Fundamental locus — constant distance from a point: {P : OP = r} = circle, centre O, radius r In Cartesian coordinates: x² + y² = r² (if O is at origin) This is literally the definition of a circle — a circle IS the locus of points equidistant from a centre. The compass draws this locus directly (one rotation of the compass arm). Option D (two parallel lines) is the locus of points at fixed distance from a line, not from a point.
Answer: B — circle, centre O, radius r
Q 18Locus at Fixed Distance from a Line
The locus of all points at a perpendicular distance d from a straight line l consists of:
(A) A single line parallel to l at distance d (B) A circle of diameter 2d centred on l (C) Two lines, each parallel to l, one on each side at distance d (D) A parabola with focus on l
Locus equidistant from a line: Points at distance d from line l: Above l: a line parallel to l, shifted up by d Below l: a line parallel to l, shifted down by d Together: TWO parallel lines, one on each side of l Common error: students draw only one parallel line, forgetting the locus exists on both sides. Option A is only half the answer. In constrained problems (e.g., "on the same side as point P"), you keep only one of the two parallels — but the full locus has both.
Answer: C — two lines, one on each side of l at distance d
Q 19Locus Equidistant from Two Points
The locus of points equidistant from two fixed points A and B is:
(A) The circle with AB as diameter (B) An ellipse with A and B as foci (C) The perpendicular bisector of AB (D) The angle bisector of the triangle formed by A, B, and the point
Perpendicular bisector = locus equidistant from two points: {P : PA = PB} = perpendicular bisector of segment AB Proof sketch: If PA = PB, triangle PAB is isosceles. The perpendicular from P to AB hits the midpoint M. So P lies on the line through M perpendicular to AB. This links Q12 (perpendicular bisector construction) to its locus interpretation. Option A (circle with AB as diameter) is the Thales locus — points from which AB subtends 90°. Option B is the definition of an ellipse (CONSTANT SUM of distances, not equal distances).
Answer: C — perpendicular bisector of AB
Q 20Locus Equidistant from Two Lines
Two straight lines PQ and RS intersect at O. The locus of points equidistant from both lines consists of:
(A) The perpendicular bisector of PQ (B) The two angle bisectors of the angles formed at O (C) A circle centred at O (D) A line parallel to both PQ and RS
Equidistant from two intersecting lines = angle bisectors: Two intersecting lines form 4 angles at O (two pairs of vertical angles): α, 180°−α, α, 180°−α The angle bisectors of the acute and obtuse angles bisect all four angles. A point equidistant from both lines lies on one of these two bisectors. Together they form a pair of perpendicular lines through O. The locus has TWO branches — both angle bisectors (they are perpendicular to each other). This mirrors Q18 (locus at fixed distance from a line has two parallel branches). The two bisectors here are the diagonals of the rhombus formed by unit points on the four rays from O.
Answer: B — the two angle bisectors at O (two perpendicular lines)
Q 21Locus — Angle in a Semicircle
The locus of points P from which a line segment AB subtends a right angle (∠APB = 90°) is:
(A) The perpendicular bisector of AB (B) An ellipse with A and B as foci (C) A circle with AB as diameter (Thales' theorem) (D) Two parallel lines on either side of AB
Thales' theorem — the semicircle locus: Thales' theorem: if P lies on a circle with diameter AB, then ∠APB = 90°. Converse: if ∠APB = 90°, then P lies on the circle with diameter AB. Locus = the full circle with diameter AB (excluding A and B themselves, since the angle is undefined at those points) This locus appears in WAEC construction questions as: "find the locus of point P such that angle APB = 90°." The answer is always: construct the circle with AB as diameter. Use a perpendicular bisector to find the centre (midpoint of AB) and use half of AB as the radius.
Answer: C — circle with AB as diameter (Thales)
Q 22Locus Summary Table
Which of the following correctly matches a locus condition to its geometric shape?
(A) Equidistant from two points → circle (B) Fixed distance from a fixed point → straight line (C) Fixed distance from a fixed line → two parallel lines (D) Equidistant from two intersecting lines → one straight line
Complete locus summary: Condition → Shape ───────────────────────────────────────────────────── Fixed distance r from point O → circle (centre O, radius r) Equidistant from points A and B → perpendicular bisector of AB (line) Fixed distance d from line l → TWO lines parallel to l (one each side) Equidistant from two lines at O → TWO angle bisectors at O (perp. to each other) Angle APB = 90° for fixed AB → circle with diameter AB (Thales) ───────────────────────────────────────────────────── Option A gives the correct shape for "fixed distance from a point" (circle) but attributes it to the wrong condition. Option D says "one straight line" — there are always TWO angle bisectors. Option C is the only correctly matched pair.
Answer: C — fixed distance from a line → two parallel lines
📐 Block 5 — Loci: Distances & Computation · Q23–Q27
Q 23Distance Condition — Circle
A and B are two points 10 cm apart. Describe the locus of all points P such that PA = 6 cm.
(A) A straight line perpendicular to AB at A (B) A circle of radius 6 cm centred at A (C) An arc of radius 6 cm on one side of AB only (D) The perpendicular bisector of AB
Single fixed-distance condition → circle: PA = 6 cm (constant), A fixed. {P : PA = 6} = circle, centre A, radius 6 cm. This circle exists completely independently of where B is. B's position only becomes relevant if a SECOND condition involves B. Option C (arc on one side only) is incorrect — the locus is the full circle, not a semicircle or arc, unless a region constraint is given. In WAEC questions, "locus" without a region qualifier means the complete geometric object.
Answer: B — circle, centre A, radius 6 cm
Q 24Two-Condition Intersection
A and B are 10 cm apart. Point P satisfies PA = 6 cm AND PB = 8 cm. How many positions are possible for P?
(A) 0 (B) 2 (C) 1 (D) Infinitely many
Intersection of two circles: Circle 1: centre A, radius 6 cm Circle 2: centre B, radius 8 cm AB = 10 cm Check triangle inequality: 6 + 8 = 14 > 10 ✓ (not too far apart) |8 − 6| = 2 < 10 ✓ (not one inside the other) Since |r₁ − r₂| < d < r₁ + r₂, the circles intersect at EXACTLY 2 points. Bonus: 6² + 8² = 36 + 64 = 100 = 10² → The two intersection points and A, B form right triangles (3-4-5 scaled by 2). In general: two circles intersect in 0, 1, or 2 points depending on the distance between centres vs. the sum/difference of radii. Here the two intersection points are the two possible positions of P.
Answer: B — 2 positions
Q 25Locus Within a Triangle
In triangle ABC, the locus of all points inside the triangle that are closer to side AB than to either BC or CA is bounded by:
(A) Circles centred at A and B (B) The perpendicular bisectors of the three sides (C) Parts of the angle bisectors of angles A and B (specifically the portions inside the triangle from each vertex toward the opposite side) (D) The medians from A and B
Region closer to one side than others — angle bisectors bound the regions: The set of points closer to line AB than to line BC is bounded by the angle bisector of angle B (the bisector of the angle between AB and BC). Similarly, the set of points closer to AB than to CA is bounded by the angle bisector of angle A (between AB and CA). The region closer to AB than to BOTH BC and CA lies between these two angle bisectors — a triangle-shaped region at the AB side. This is a standard WAEC locus-region problem. The three angle bisectors divide the interior of the triangle into three regions, each closest to one side. The boundaries are always portions of the angle bisectors at the vertices adjacent to that side.
Answer: C — angle bisectors at A and B
Q 26Locus — Constant Distance from Two Lines
A point P moves so that it is always 3 cm from a straight line XY. Separately, point Q moves so that it is always equidistant from two parallel lines, each 6 cm apart. Describe the loci of P and Q respectively.
(A) P: a circle of radius 3 cm; Q: the midline parallel to both (B) P: two lines parallel to XY at 3 cm on each side; Q: the midline (one line) parallel to both, at 3 cm from each (C) P: a single line 3 cm from XY; Q: two lines, one at 2 cm and one at 4 cm from each (D) P: a parabola; Q: the perpendicular bisector of the segment joining the two lines
Two separate loci: Locus of P: {P : distance from XY = 3 cm} = two lines parallel to XY, one 3 cm above, one 3 cm below. Locus of Q: Two parallel lines 6 cm apart — call them l₁ and l₂. {Q : distance to l₁ = distance to l₂} = the single midline l₃, which is parallel to both and lies exactly 3 cm from each. (Only ONE midline, because the two original lines are parallel — not intersecting. If they intersected, the locus would be two angle bisectors.) Key: "equidistant from two parallel lines" → one line (the midline). "Equidistant from two intersecting lines" → two lines (the angle bisectors). The parallelism changes the shape of the locus.
Answer: B
Q 27Locus — Distance from a Point and a Line
A point P is 5 cm from a fixed point F and also 5 cm from a fixed straight line l (where F is 10 cm from l). Approximately how many positions are possible for P?
(A) 0 (B) 1 (C) 2 (D) 4
Circle meets two parallel lines: Locus 1: {P : PF = 5} = circle, centre F, radius 5. Locus 2: {P : distance from l = 5} = two lines parallel to l: l₁ at 5 cm above l, and l₂ at 5 cm below l. F is 10 cm from l (on one side). Distance from F to l₁ (5 cm above l) = 10 − 5 = 5 cm → circle intersects l₁ in 2 points. Distance from F to l₂ (5 cm below l) = 10 + 5 = 15 cm → circle radius only 5, so no intersection. Total valid positions: 2. This problem illustrates how to combine two loci — find each separately, then find the intersections. The key calculation is comparing the radius of the circle with the distance from the centre to each parallel line.
Answer: C — 2 positions

Q 28Combined Loci — WAEC Type
Points A and B are 8 cm apart. Shade the region containing all points P such that PA ≤ 5 cm AND PB ≤ 5 cm. Describe this region.
(A) The interior of a circle of radius 5 cm centred at the midpoint of AB (B) The intersection (lens-shaped region) of the disk of radius 5 cm centred at A and the disk of radius 5 cm centred at B (C) The strip between the two parallel lines through A and B perpendicular to AB (D) The interior of a square with side AB
Intersection of two disk regions: {P : PA ≤ 5} = disk (filled circle) of radius 5, centre A {P : PB ≤ 5} = disk (filled circle) of radius 5, centre B AB = 8 cm; r₁ = r₂ = 5 cm. Since |r₁ − r₂| = 0 < AB = 8 < r₁ + r₂ = 10, the two circles intersect in two points, creating a lens (vesica piscis shape). The required region is the overlap — the lens-shaped area inside both circles. Boundary: two circular arcs, one centred at A and one at B. The construction: draw both circles, shade the region enclosed by both arcs. This is the standard WAEC shading question. Option A (circle at midpoint) is a common guess but is wrong — the midpoint circle does not match both conditions unless r = 4 cm (half of AB).
Answer: B — lens (intersection of two disks)
Q 29Three-Condition Loci Problem
In square ABCD with side 6 cm, a point P must satisfy all three conditions: (i) PA ≤ PB   (ii) PA ≤ PC   (iii) PA ≤ PD. The region satisfying all three conditions is bounded by which constructions?
(A) Three circles centred at A, B, and C (B) Parts of the perpendicular bisectors of AB, AC (diagonal), and AD — the region closer to A than to any other vertex (C) The angle bisectors from each vertex of the square (D) A circle inscribed in the square
Voronoi region of vertex A in the square: PA ≤ PB ↔ P is on A's side of the perpendicular bisector of AB PA ≤ PD ↔ P is on A's side of the perpendicular bisector of AD PA ≤ PC ↔ P is on A's side of the perpendicular bisector of diagonal AC The three bisectors meet inside the square. The region satisfying all three is a triangle/polygon adjacent to vertex A. This is the Voronoi cell of A among the four vertices — the set of all points closer to A than to any other vertex. For a square, it is a smaller square occupying one quarter of the original, bounded by the midlines (perpendicular bisectors of each side) and the perpendicular bisector of the diagonal.
Answer: B — perpendicular bisectors of AB, AD, and diagonal AC
Q 30Full Construction Problem — WAEC Style
Using a ruler and compass only, describe a step-by-step construction to find all points P such that: P is equidistant from two points X and Y (XY = 10 cm), AND P is equidistant from two lines XZ and YZ that meet at Z at an angle of 60°.
(A) Draw the perpendicular bisector of XY only — P must lie on it (B) Bisect angle XZY only — P must lie on the bisector (C) Construct both: (1) perpendicular bisector of XY, and (2) bisector of angle XZY. The intersection(s) of these two loci give the required point(s) P (D) P cannot be found without measuring angle Z
Combined loci problem — intersection of two constructions: Condition 1: equidistant from X and Y → locus: perpendicular bisector of XY (a straight line) Condition 2: equidistant from lines XZ and YZ → locus: angle bisector of angle XZY (a ray from Z) Both loci are straight lines (in this case). Their intersection is a single point P (if they are not parallel). Construction steps: 1. Draw XY = 10 cm. 2. Construct perpendicular bisector of XY → call it l₁. 3. Construct the 60° angle at Z (using the equilateral triangle method). 4. Bisect angle XZY → ray l₂ from Z. 5. Find intersection of l₁ and l₂ → this is P. The key principle: when P must satisfy two independent locus conditions, construct each locus separately, then find their intersection. Each construction reduces the problem by one degree of freedom: from plane → line → point.
Answer: C — perpendicular bisector of XY ∩ angle bisector of XZY
