Module One — Fitness (50%)

The Calendar

Fourteen weeks, four tournament stages. Load is plotted, not guessed — every cell is a day's training intensity (0–10). Tap a cell to cycle it. The threshold line above reacts to the week you're viewing.

Dribble allowance this match: 7 Showing Week 1
Rest / Recovery Base — technical Build — match sharpness Peak — match week
Anti-injury, maximum-gain rule: no more than seven dribbles in open play across a full match — unless the match is a semi-final, where the ceiling lifts to ten. Dribbling is the highest-load individual action a body performs in football: deceleration, change of direction, contact absorption, repeated. Rationing it isn't caution for its own sake — it's where the 50% and the 30% actually meet. A player who has done the fitness work can afford to spend one of those seven dribbles on something decisive, rather than something habitual.
Module Two — Doctrine

Five Classes
Of The Game

Not formations, not positions — the five recurring situations every player, in every shirt, will live inside repeatedly. Learn these and the position becomes a detail.

Class 01

The First Touch Under Pressure

Receiving

The ball arrives with someone close behind you. Everything that follows — the next three seconds of the entire match — is decided by where this touch goes, not by what you do after it. Most goals and most turnovers begin here, unwatched.

GK — distribution under press. CB — first line under press. FW — back to goal, defender touching.
Class 02

The Recovery Run

Defensive Transition

You've just lost the ball. The next five seconds are the cheapest defensive work a team does all match — cheaper than a tackle, cheaper than a block — if the run starts immediately and at the right angle, not the fastest one.

FB — recovering wide overlap. MID — screening the second pass. WG — tracking the underlap.
Class 03

The Third Man

Combination Play

Two players are involved in a pass. A third — unmarked, often unnoticed — is the actual point of it. Reading when you are that third man, and moving before the first pass is even played, is the difference between supporting play and directing it.

CM — the most common home. FB — overlapping underneath. ST — dropping to be the link.
Class 04

The Box Entry

Final Third

Possession outside the box is patient. Possession inside it is final. The class is about the half-second of recognising which one you're in — and that a touch that was acceptable five yards earlier is now too slow.

ST — timing the run across the line. WG — the cutback decision. CM — the late arrival.
Class 05

The Last Five Minutes

Game Management

Fitness, skill, teammates and coaching all converge here under fatigue, and the doctrine's ratios stop being theory. A tired probabilistic player still makes the simple pass. A tired romantic player goes looking for the moment — and sometimes finds it, but rarely on purpose.

ALL — discipline over heroics, almost always. EXC — score margin, not personal feeling, decides which.
Module Three — Skill (its own 100%, once fitness clears)

The Periodic
Table Of Moves

Eighteen essential moves, grouped by what they're for rather than how they look. Filter by group to study one family at a time — every position draws from at least two of the four.

Ground Control — first touch, dribble, pass Aerial & Strike — finishing, heading, delivery Defensive Actions — the tackle family Transition — the connective moves
On the gate: this table is the 30%, and the 30% is gated by the 50%. A player with elite ground control but no fitness base will lose the ball in the 70th minute exactly when they had it in the 10th — not because the skill disappeared, but because the legs that delivered it did. Train the table in order of your weakest group, but train the calendar first.
Module Four — The Other 20%

What You
Don't Train

Teammate level and coaching aren't trainable by the player — but a probabilistic player accounts for them instead of resenting them. This is the difference between adapting to context and waiting for it to change.

10

Teammate Level

The same pass into the same space is a goal with one teammate's movement and a turnover with another's. You don't control this — but a probabilistic player adjusts the weight, timing, and even the choice of pass based on who's making the run, every time, without complaint.

10

Coaching

Instructions, formations, substitution timing — set by someone else, for the team, not for you specifically. The probabilistic player executes the framework precisely, because precision inside someone else's structure is still inside your control. The romantic player waits for a framework that suits them.
