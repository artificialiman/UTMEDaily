Module One — Fitness (50%)

The Calendar

Dribble allowance this match: 7 Showing Week 1

Anti-injury, maximum-gain rule: no more than seven dribbles in open play across a full match — unless the match is a semi-final, where the ceiling lifts to ten. Dribbling is the highest-load individual action a body performs in football: deceleration, change of direction, contact absorption, repeated. Rationing it isn't caution for its own sake — it's where the 50% and the 30% actually meet. A player who has done the fitness work can afford to spend one of those seven dribbles on something decisive, rather than something habitual.
Module Two — Skill (its own 100%, once fitness clears)

Four Subclasses.
Four Tables.

The 30% doesn't sit as one block — it splits into four families, and each family has earned its own periodic table. Read them in order if you're starting from zero: vision changes what you see, protection changes what you keep, carries change where it goes, and shooting is what all three were for.

Subclass 01

Vision & Presence

9 elements · 2 families

The reactive family fires before the ball arrives — fast, low-effort, almost involuntary once trained, the way a reactive metal doesn't wait to be asked. The touch family is the output discipline: if the scan was right, one or two touches is all the situation needed. Both families are light on the body, which makes this subclass the best entry point for re-onboarding after injury or for a player just starting.

Scanning — reads space, gaps, overloads, angles before contact 1–2 Touch — output discipline, zero/low contact, re-onboarding safe
Why it's first: a correct scan turns a "1-touch" situation into an easy two-touch one, and a missed scan turns an easy two-touch into a desperate three. The touch count isn't the skill — it's the receipt the scan prints.
Subclass 02

Ball Protection

8 elements · 2 families

Two questions, asked in order. How do you keep the ball when someone's close — that's Control. Why are you keeping it right now — that's the 1v1 family, and the reason changes the choice. Shielding to wait for a runner is a different shape than shielding to draw a foul, even if the body position looks identical from the stands.

Control — the body mechanics of keeping it 1v1 — the tactical reason you're keeping it
The tell: watch the eyes, not the feet. A control variant aimed at the 1v1 reason "hold for support" keeps checking over the shoulder for the runner. The same shape aimed at "draw the foul" stops checking entirely — because the contact is the point.
Subclass 03

Sprints & Carries

8 elements · 2 families

One duel, two sides of it. These aren't moves so much as roles a player settles into during a sprint contest — the archetype tells you what the body is optimising for in that half-second, on either side of the ball. Most players are strong in one attacking and one defending archetype, rarely all four.

Attacking — the player carrying or beating Defending — the player stopping or recovering
The 7-dribble link: The Eliminator and The Engine are the two archetypes that spend dribbles fastest. If your training week shows a player drifting toward both at once, the calendar's dribble ceiling is the thing keeping the body honest — not the coach.
Subclass 04

Shooting & Kicking

7 elements · 1 gradient

This table has no two families — it's a single gradient. At the top is the impossible: receive, one touch, goal, every time. Nobody lives there. Below it, in descending order of how close they get, are the techniques that exist precisely because the ball travels faster and less predictably than the player chasing it — every tier down is a different answer to that one problem.

The Impossible — the unreachable standard Closest — first-time, minimal adjustment Craft — the ball didn't arrive clean, adapt Last Resort — distance, power, or improvisation over precision
Why this is last: shooting is what the other three subclasses were building toward. Vision finds the angle, protection survives long enough to reach it, the carry closes the distance — and then the ball, which has never once in the history of the game arrived perfectly, has to be turned into a shot anyway. The gap between Tier 0 and everything below it is football's actual beauty. Closing it by a few centimetres, repeatedly, is the whole craft.
Module Three — The Other 20%

What You
Don't Train

Teammate level and coaching aren't trainable by the player — but a probabilistic player accounts for them instead of resenting them. This is the difference between adapting to context and waiting for it to change.

10

Teammate Level

The same pass into the same space is a goal with one teammate's movement and a turnover with another's. You don't control this — but a probabilistic player adjusts the weight, timing, and even the choice of pass based on who's making the run, every time, without complaint.

10

Coaching

Instructions, formations, substitution timing — set by someone else, for the team, not for you specifically. The probabilistic player executes the framework precisely, because precision inside someone else's structure is still inside your control. The romantic player waits for a framework that suits them.
