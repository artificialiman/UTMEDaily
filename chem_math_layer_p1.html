01 · Stoichiometry & Mole Concept
n = m / M
Moles from mass
n = moles · m = mass (g) · M = molar mass (g/mol)
core M confusion: element vs compound
n = V / 22.4
Moles from volume (STP)
STP = 0°C, 1 atm. WAEC sometimes uses 22.4, UTME occasionally 22.4 or molar volume given.
STP confirm molar volume given in question
n = N / NA
Moles from particles
NA = 6.02 × 10²³ mol⁻¹ (Avogadro). N = number of particles (atoms, molecules, ions).
Avogadro ions ≠ molecules — count carefully
% = (part / whole) × 100
Percentage composition
part = mass of element in 1 mol of compound. Whole = molar mass of compound.
composition
actual yield / theoretical yield × 100
Percentage yield
Theoretical yield from stoichiometry. Actual from experiment. Never exceeds 100%.
yield limiting reagent must be found first
empirical → molecular:
n = Mmol / Memp
Empirical to molecular formula
Find empirical formula from % composition first. Then scale by n using given molar mass.
empirical divide % by Ar, then divide by smallest
02 · Concentration & Solutions
C = n / V
Molar concentration
C in mol/dm³ · n in mol · V in dm³ (= litres). Convert cm³ ÷ 1000.
core cm³ → dm³ most common error
C1V1 = C2V2
Dilution law
Moles conserved on dilution. Units must match on each side.
dilution
CAVA / nA = CBVB / nB
Titration formula
nA, nB = mole ratio from balanced equation. Most common UTME calculation.
titration read mole ratio from equation first acid-base, redox, precipitation variants
mass conc. (g/dm³) = C × M
Mass concentration
Convert molar → mass concentration. M = molar mass of solute.
conversion
% w/v = (mass solute / vol solution) × 100
Weight/volume percentage
mass in g, volume in mL (cm³). Common in lab/pharmacy contexts.
practical
mole fraction: χA = nA / (nA + nB)
Mole fraction
Dimensionless. Sum of all mole fractions = 1. Feeds into vapour pressure and Raoult's law.
colligative
03 · Gas Laws
PV = nRT
Ideal gas equation
R = 8.314 J/mol·K. T must be in Kelvin. P in Pa, V in m³ — OR use consistent units throughout.
core T = °C + 273 always unit consistency critical
P1V1/T1 = P2V2/T2
Combined gas law
Use when two of P, V, T change. Lock the constant variable (e.g. constant P → Boyle's drops out).
combined identify what's constant before substituting
Component laws
P1V1 = P2V2
Boyle's law (constant T)
P ∝ 1/V. Graph: P vs 1/V is linear. P vs V is hyperbola.
isothermal
V1/T1 = V2/T2
Charles's law (constant P)
V ∝ T. Graph: V vs T (K) is linear through origin.
isobaric
P1/T1 = P2/T2
Gay-Lussac's law (constant V)
P ∝ T. Relevant for sealed containers, autoclave, aerosol questions.
isochoric
Ptotal = PA + PB + PC...
Dalton's law of partial pressures
PA = χA × Ptotal. Common in gas collection over water: Pgas = Ptotal − Pwater vapour.
partial pressure subtract water vapour pressure in collection questions
rateA/rateB = √(MB/MA)
Graham's law of diffusion
Lighter gas diffuses faster. Rate ∝ 1/√M. Used for identifying unknown gas molar mass.
diffusion ratio inverted — heavier under root on top
V ∝ n (constant T, P)
Avogadro's law
Equal volumes of gases at same T and P contain equal moles. Foundation of molar volume = 22.4 L at STP.
molar volume
04 · Thermochemistry & Energetics
q = mcΔT
Heat absorbed/released
m = mass (g) · c = specific heat capacity · ΔT = Tfinal − Tinitial. For water c = 4.18 J/g·K.
core sign: exothermic q is negative for system
ΔH = Hproducts − Hreactants
Enthalpy change
ΔH < 0: exothermic. ΔH > 0: endothermic. State symbols matter — ΔH°f refers to standard state.
enthalpy standard conditions: 298 K, 1 atm
ΔHrxn = ΣΔH°f(products) − ΣΔH°f(reactants)
Hess's law (formation route)
ΔH°f of element in standard state = 0. Most UTME Hess questions use 2–3 step cycles.
Hess flip equation → flip sign multiply equation → multiply ΔH
ΔH = ΣBE(broken) − ΣBE(formed)
Bond energy route
Energy in (bonds broken) − energy out (bonds formed). Positive BE = energy released when bond forms.
bond energy breaking bonds = endothermic always
Born-Haber cycle:
ΔHlatt = ΔHf − (ΔHsub + IE + ½BE + EA)
Lattice energy (Born-Haber)
Apply Hess's law around the cycle. Each step has a defined sign convention. WAEC only.
WAECionic
05 · Chemical Equilibrium
Kc = [C]c[D]d / [A]a[B]b
Equilibrium constant (Kc)
For aA + bB ⇌ cC + dD. Products over reactants. Pure solids and liquids excluded.
core solids/liquids omitted from expression reverse reaction: K' = 1/K
Kp = Kc(RT)Δn
Kp from Kc
Δn = moles gas products − moles gas reactants. If Δn = 0, Kp = Kc.
gas equilibrium
Q vs K: direction of shift
Reaction quotient Q
Q < K → reaction shifts right (forward). Q > K → shifts left (reverse). Q = K → equilibrium.
Le Chatelier temperature change alters K itself — not just position
degree of dissociation α:
Ka ≈ Cα² (weak acid, small α)
Weak acid dissociation
Valid when α << 1 (< 5%). C = initial concentration. Gives [H⁺] = √(Ka·C).
weak acid
06 · Acids, Bases & pH
pH = −log[H⁺]
pH definition
[H⁺] in mol/dm³. pH + pOH = 14 at 25°C. Each unit = 10× change in [H⁺].
core pH < 7 acid · = 7 neutral · > 7 base at 25°C
pOH = −log[OH⁻]
pH + pOH = 14
pOH and the water constant
Kw = [H⁺][OH⁻] = 1×10⁻¹⁴ at 25°C. pKw = 14.
Kw
pH = pKa + log([A⁻]/[HA])
Henderson-Hasselbalch (buffer)
For buffer solutions. pH = pKa when [A⁻] = [HA]. WAEC; occasionally implied in UTME.
buffer ratio of conjugate base to weak acid
Ka × Kb = Kw
Conjugate pair relationship
Strong conjugate → weak partner. pKa + pKb = 14.
conjugate
07 · Electrochemistry
Q = It
Charge
Q in coulombs · I in amperes · t in seconds. Foundation of all Faraday calculations.
core
m = (Q × M) / (F × z)
Faraday's law — mass deposited
F = 96500 C/mol. z = number of electrons transferred (valency). M = molar mass of substance.
core z from half-equation, not periodic table valency directly confirm F value given in question
E°cell = E°cathode − E°anode
Standard cell potential
Cathode = reduction (more positive E°). Anode = oxidation. Spontaneous if E°cell > 0.
EMF reduction potentials given — anode reaction is reversed
OIL RIG
Oxidation/reduction mnemonic
Oxidation Is Loss of electrons. Reduction Is Gain. Applies to both ionic half-equations and oxidation state changes.
redox
oxidation state rules:
O = −2, H = +1, sum = charge
Oxidation state assignment
Exceptions: O in peroxides = −1, O with F = +2. H in metal hydrides = −1. Atom in element = 0.
oxidation state peroxide exception common trap
08 · Reaction Kinetics
rate = k[A]m[B]n
Rate equation
k = rate constant. m, n = orders (found experimentally, not from equation). Overall order = m + n.
core orders NOT from stoichiometric coefficients
t½ = 0.693 / k
Half-life (first order)
Constant half-life = first order. t½ independent of initial concentration. Used in radioactive decay.
first orderradioactivity
ln[A] = ln[A]0 − kt
First order integrated rate law
Plot ln[A] vs t → straight line, slope = −k. Used to verify order and find k.
first order
Ea from Arrhenius:
k = Ae−Ea/RT
Arrhenius equation
Ea = activation energy. A = frequency factor. Higher T → larger k → faster rate. Catalyst lowers Ea.
activation energy catalyst lowers Ea, does NOT change ΔH
09 · Colligative Properties
ΔTb = Kb × m
ΔTf = Kf × m
Boiling point elevation / Freezing point depression
m = molality (mol solute / kg solvent). Kb, Kf = constants for solvent. Electrolytes: multiply by van't Hoff factor i.
colligative molality not molarity i = number of ions for electrolytes
π = iMRT
Osmotic pressure
π in Pa or atm. M = molarity. i = van't Hoff factor. R = 8.314. T in K.
osmosis
PA = χA × P°A
Raoult's law (vapour pressure)
Vapour pressure of solution lower than pure solvent. Non-volatile solute → vapour pressure lowering.
vapour pressure
10 · Radioactivity
N = N0 × (½)t/t½
Radioactive decay — amount remaining
N0 = initial amount. t = elapsed time. t½ = half-life. Works for mass, activity or number of atoms.
core ensure t and t½ in same units
α: ⁴₂He · β⁻: ⁰₋₁e · β⁺: ⁰₊₁e · γ: ⁰₀γ
Decay particle notation
α decay: Z−2, A−4. β⁻ decay: Z+1, A unchanged. β⁺ decay: Z−1, A unchanged. γ: no change.
decay modes mass number conserved in all decay
nuclear binding energy:
E = Δm × c²
Mass-energy equivalence
Δm = mass defect. c = 3×10⁸ m/s. 1 amu = 931 MeV. WAEC; conceptual only at UTME.
WAECnuclear
11 · Organic Chemistry — Quantitative
degree of unsaturation:
DoU = (2C + 2 + N − H − X) / 2
Index of hydrogen deficiency
DoU = 0: saturated. 1: one ring or double bond. 2: two rings/double bonds or one triple bond. 4: benzene ring.
organic O not in formula — doesn't affect DoU
saponification value ∝ 1/Mfat
Saponification (qualitative relationship)
Higher saponification value → shorter chain fatty acids → lower molar mass fat. Mostly conceptual at UTME.
fats & oils
n(H₂) added = number of C=C bonds
Hydrogenation stoichiometry
1 mol H₂ per C=C double bond. Used to find number of unsaturated bonds from volume of H₂ absorbed.
unsaturation
12 · Atomic Structure — Quantitative
E = hf = hc/λ
Photon energy
h = 6.63×10⁻³⁴ J·s. f = frequency. λ = wavelength. c = 3×10⁸ m/s. c = fλ.
quantum λ in metres not nm for SI units
max electrons per shell: 2n²
Shell capacity
n=1: 2, n=2: 8, n=3: 18, n=4: 32. Sub-shell filling: s(2) p(6) d(10) f(14).
electron config
isotope: same Z, different A
A = Z + N
Atomic number / mass number
Z = protons. N = neutrons. A = mass number. Relative atomic mass = weighted average of isotope masses.
isotopes RAM calculation: Σ(mass × abundance)/100
