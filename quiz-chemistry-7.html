Question 2 of 40
An element has atomic number 24 (chromium, Cr). Its actual electron configuration differs from the Aufbau prediction. The correct configuration is
Show Answer & Explanation
✓ Correct answer: D — [Ar] 3d⁵ 4s¹
Aufbau predicts [Ar] 3d⁴ 4s². However, a half-filled 3d⁵ has extra exchange-energy stability. One electron migrates from 4s → 3d: actual Cr = [Ar] 3d⁵ 4s¹. Similarly, Cu = [Ar] 3d¹⁰ 4s¹ (full 3d preferred). These are the two most-tested Aufbau exceptions in JAMB. The rule: half-filled and fully filled d subshells are unusually stable.
Question 3 of 40
In the hydrogen energy-level diagram, an electron falls from n=3 to n=1. The radiation emitted belongs to which series and region of the electromagnetic spectrum?
Show Answer & Explanation
✓ Correct answer: B — Lyman series; ultraviolet
Lyman series: transitions TO n=1 → ultraviolet radiation (high energy, short wavelength). Balmer series: transitions TO n=2 → visible/near-UV. Paschen: TO n=3 → infrared. The n=3→n=1 emission is in the Lyman series. Energy = 13.6(1/1² − 1/3²) = 13.6 × 8/9 ≈ 12.1 eV → UV photon. Students commonly confuse Lyman/Balmer — remember: Lyman = Level 1 = Lowest (UV).
Question 4 of 40
Which quantum number describes the SHAPE of an atomic orbital?
Show Answer & Explanation
✓ Correct answer: B — Azimuthal / angular momentum quantum number (l)
l (azimuthal quantum number): l=0 → s orbital (spherical); l=1 → p orbital (dumbbell); l=2 → d orbital (cloverleaf). n gives the energy shell/size. mₗ gives orientation in space. mₛ gives electron spin (±½). Shape = l. Orientation = mₗ. These two are the most commonly swapped in JAMB.
Question 5 of 40
From the periodic trend graph, what is the correct description of how atomic radius and electronegativity change across Period 2 from Li to F?
Show Answer & Explanation
✓ Correct answer: D — Atomic radius decreases; electronegativity increases
Across a period (left to right): nuclear charge increases (more protons), but electrons are added to the SAME shell — increased nuclear pull shrinks the atom → atomic radius DECREASES. More protons also increase the atom's ability to attract bonding electrons → electronegativity INCREASES. These two trends are the most tested graph-reading items in JAMB Periodicity.
Question 6 of 40
The isotopes ¹²C and ¹⁴C have the same chemical properties because they
Show Answer & Explanation
✓ Correct answer: D — Have the same number of protons and electrons
Chemical properties are determined by the number of ELECTRONS (and their arrangement), which equals the number of protons for a neutral atom. Both ¹²C and ¹⁴C have 6 protons → 6 electrons → same electronic configuration → same chemical behaviour. They differ in neutron count (6 vs 8) and therefore mass. ¹⁴C is radioactive; ¹²C is stable — chemistry is the same.

Question 8 of 40
From the Maxwell-Boltzmann diagram, why does increasing temperature from T₁ to T₂ increase the rate of reaction?
Show Answer & Explanation
✓ Correct answer: D — A greater proportion of molecules have energy ≥ Eₐ at T₂
At higher T₂, the distribution flattens and shifts right (peak moves to higher energy). The shaded area beyond Eₐ is LARGER at T₂ — a greater fraction of molecules possess kinetic energy ≥ Eₐ and can react upon collision. Eₐ itself does NOT change with temperature (that's what a catalyst does). Total molecules in a sealed container are constant. The rate increase is entirely due to the larger fraction exceeding Eₐ.
Question 9 of 40
For the equilibrium: PCl₅(g) ⇌ PCl₃(g) + Cl₂(g) Kc = 0.04 mol/dm³ at 250°C. If [PCl₅] = 0.2 mol/dm³ and [PCl₃] = [Cl₂] = 0.1 mol/dm³, the reaction quotient Qc is
Show Answer & Explanation
✓ Correct answer: B — Qc = 0.05; reaction shifts LEFT
Qc = [PCl₃][Cl₂]/[PCl₅] = (0.1)(0.1)/(0.2) = 0.01/0.2 = 0.05. Since Qc (0.05) > Kc (0.04), the system has too many products relative to equilibrium → reaction shifts LEFT (reverse) to reduce products and increase PCl₅. Memory aid: Q > K → shift left; Q < K → shift right; Q = K → at equilibrium.
Question 10 of 40
Using the Hess's Law cycle shown, ΔH₂ = −111 kJ/mol (C→CO) and ΔH₁ = −394 kJ/mol (C→CO₂). The enthalpy change for CO(g) + ½O₂(g) → CO₂(g) (ΔH₃) is
Show Answer & Explanation
✓ Correct answer: C — −283 kJ/mol
Hess's Law: ΔH₁ = ΔH₂ + ΔH₃ → ΔH₃ = ΔH₁ − ΔH₂ = −394 − (−111) = −394 + 111 = −283 kJ/mol. This is the standard enthalpy of combustion of carbon monoxide. Trap: students add rather than subtract, getting −505 kJ/mol (option C). The cycle reads: going via the indirect route (ΔH₂ then ΔH₃) must equal the direct route (ΔH₁).
Question 11 of 40
For an exothermic reaction with activation energy Eₐ, which statement is correct regarding the effect of a catalyst?
Show Answer & Explanation
✓ Correct answer: A — The catalyst lowers Eₐ for BOTH forward and reverse reactions by the same amount
A catalyst provides an alternative pathway with a LOWER activation energy for BOTH directions — it affects the rate of reaching equilibrium, not the position of equilibrium. ΔH (the energy difference between reactants and products) is unchanged — the same energy landscape, just a lower barrier in both directions. Option B is wrong because a catalyst lowers both Eₐ(fwd) and Eₐ(rev) equally. Option D confuses catalyst with equilibrium shift (only concentration/pressure/temperature changes affect position).
Question 12 of 40
The S-shaped titration curve shown is for a strong acid (HCl) against a strong base (NaOH). What is the pH at the equivalence point, and which indicator is most appropriate?
Show Answer & Explanation
✓ Correct answer: A — pH = 7; either methyl orange or phenolphthalein is suitable
Strong acid + strong base → salt (NaCl) + water at equivalence point → pH = 7. The steep vertical portion of the S-curve near equivalence spans roughly pH 3–11 for strong acid/base systems, so BOTH methyl orange (changes 3.1–4.4) and phenolphthalein (changes 8.2–10) fall within the steep section and give a sharp colour change at near-identical volumes. Either is suitable. This contrasts with weak acid/strong base (use phenolphthalein only) or strong acid/weak base (methyl orange only).
Question 13 of 40
At constant temperature, the rate constant k for a reaction doubles when temperature increases from 25°C to 35°C. This implies that the reaction
Show Answer & Explanation
✓ Correct answer: D — Has a moderate activation energy and obeys the Arrhenius equation
The Arrhenius equation: k = A·e^(−Eₐ/RT). Doubling k per 10°C rise is a classic rule-of-thumb for reactions with moderate Eₐ (~50–80 kJ/mol). Zero Eₐ would mean k is temperature-independent. Reaction order is separate from the temperature dependence of k. Equilibrium is always reached at any temperature (position may differ). The doubling behaviour is the Arrhenius relationship in action.

Question 15 of 40
From the four C₄H₁₀O structural isomers shown, which compound cannot be oxidised by acidified K₂Cr₂O₇ and will NOT turn the dichromate solution green?
Show Answer & Explanation
✓ Correct answer: A — Diethyl ether (C)

Question 16 of 40
But-2-ene reacts with HBr. According to Markovnikov's rule, the MAJOR product is
Show Answer & Explanation
✓ Correct answer: A — 2-bromobutane
But-2-ene is CH₃–CH=CH–CH₃. The double bond is symmetric (both carbons have one CH₃ and one H). Both carbons of the double bond are equivalent → H⁺ adds to either carbon equally → Br⁻ goes to the same carbon (C2 or C3, which are equivalent positions in this symmetric alkene). Product = 2-bromobutane (CH₃CHBrCH₂CH₃). Note: 3-bromobutane does not exist as a distinct compound from 2-bromobutane here. This is also 2-bromobutane regardless of labelling direction. The symmetric nature of but-2-ene makes the Markovnikov question a subtlety trap.
Question 17 of 40
The three benzene representations are shown. Which statement about the TRUE structure of benzene is CORRECT?
Show Answer & Explanation
✓ Correct answer: B — The delocalised structure (C) best represents benzene — all C–C bonds are equal in length and strength
The true benzene structure is neither Kekulé I nor II — it is a resonance HYBRID in which all six C–C bonds are equivalent (bond order 1.5). Bond length 140 pm is between C–C (154) and C=C (134). The circle inside the hexagon (C) represents the 6 delocalized π electrons spread over the ring. Benzene does NOT oscillate between two structures — the actual electron density is intermediate and uniform. This is why benzene undergoes substitution (not addition) reactions — the aromatic ring is preserved.
Question 18 of 40
Which of the following is the correct test to distinguish between an aldehyde and a ketone?
Show Answer & Explanation
✓ Correct answer: C — Add Fehling's solution — aldehydes give a brick-red precipitate; ketones give no change
Aldehydes are reducing agents (have a –CHO group) and reduce Fehling's solution (blue Cu²⁺) to a brick-red Cu₂O precipitate. Ketones do NOT reduce Fehling's solution — the carbonyl carbon is flanked by two carbon groups and cannot be oxidised under mild conditions. Tollens' reagent (silver mirror) is an alternative. Bromine water is decolourised by alkenes and some reducing compounds but not this specific aldehyde/ketone distinction. NaOH and phenolphthalein do not distinguish them.
Question 19 of 40
In the esterification shown, the –OH that forms water comes from which reactant, and what type of reaction is this?
Show Answer & Explanation
✓ Correct answer: D — From the carboxylic acid; condensation (elimination) reaction
In esterification, the O–H eliminated as water comes from the CARBOXYLIC ACID (the –OH of –COOH). The alcohol contributes its –H. Using ¹⁸O labelling experiments confirm this. The reaction type is condensation (a specific elimination) because a small molecule (H₂O) is expelled as the ester bond (–COO–) forms. Not addition (no new bonds from an unsaturated compound). Not redox (no oxidation state change).

Question 21 of 40
Which of the following correctly describes the difference between saponification and esterification?
Show Answer & Explanation
✓ Correct answer: A — Saponification is base hydrolysis of fats/esters to give soap (fatty acid salt) + glycerol; esterification forms esters from alcohol + acid
Saponification: triglyceride + NaOH (or KOH) → glycerol + sodium/potassium salts of fatty acids (soap). The base causes irreversible hydrolysis of ester bonds in fats. Esterification: alcohol + carboxylic acid ⇌ ester + water (reversible, acid-catalysed). The key distinctions: (1) saponification uses BASE, esterification uses ACID catalyst; (2) saponification is irreversible, esterification reversible; (3) saponification starts with fat (triglyceride), esterification from individual alcohol + acid.
Question 22 of 40
In the chromatogram shown, unknown mixture D produces two spots that coincide exactly with reference spots A and C. This means that mixture D contains
Show Answer & Explanation
✓ Correct answer: A — Substances A and C
In paper chromatography, a substance is identified by its Rf value (spot position). If spots from the unknown D align exactly with reference spots A and C, then D contains A and C. Spot coincidence at the same Rf position strongly suggests the same substance (confirmed by running multiple solvent systems). Spots for B and D at different Rf would not overlap — B is ruled out. What is the general formula for a homologous series of ALKYNES, and what is the simplest member?
Show Answer & Explanation
✓ Correct answer: B — CnH₂n−2; ethyne (C₂H₂)
Alkynes (one triple bond): CnH₂n−2 (two fewer H than alkenes due to triple bond). Simplest alkyne: ethyne C₂H₂ (n=2). CnH₂n = alkenes; CnH₂n+2 = alkanes. Question 24 of 40
In the SN2 mechanism shown, the rate equation is rate = k[HO⁻][CH₃Br]. If the concentration of HO⁻ is doubled while [CH₃Br] is halved, the rate will
Show Answer & Explanation
✓ Correct answer: C — Remain the same
Rate = k[HO⁻][CH₃Br]. New rate = k(2[HO⁻])(½[CH₃Br]) = k × 2 × ½ × [HO⁻][CH₃Br] = k[HO⁻][CH₃Br]. The factors cancel: rate unchanged. This is a classic SN2/rate-law calculation that uses the bimolecular nature of the reaction. Students who compute the changes individually rather than together often err. The equal and opposite changes cancel perfectly.

Question 26 of 40
In the electrolysis of molten NaCl shown, which statement is CORRECT about the electrode processes?
Show Answer & Explanation
✓ Correct answer: D — Na⁺ is reduced at the cathode (Na⁺ + e⁻ → Na) and Cl⁻ is oxidised at the anode (2Cl⁻ → Cl₂ + 2e⁻)
Cathode (negative): cations (Na⁺) migrate to it and GAIN electrons (reduction): Na⁺ + e⁻ → Na(l). Anode (positive): anions (Cl⁻) migrate to it and LOSE electrons (oxidation): 2Cl⁻ → Cl₂(g) + 2e⁻. "Oil Rig" — Oxidation Is Loss at the anode, Reduction Is Gain at the cathode. Oxygen would only form if water were present (dilute aqueous solution). Molten NaCl is anhydrous.

Question 30 of 40
From the phase diagram of water, what is unusual about the solid-liquid boundary line (compared to most other substances)?
Show Answer & Explanation
✓ Correct answer: A — It slopes to the LEFT (negative gradient), meaning increased pressure LOWERS the melting point of ice
Water is anomalous: the solid-liquid boundary has a NEGATIVE slope (leans left). For most substances the line leans right (increased pressure raises melting point). For water, increased pressure LOWERS the melting point — ice melts under pressure. This is because liquid water is DENSER than ice (water expands on freezing). By Le Chatelier: increasing pressure favours the denser phase (liquid) → ice melts. This anomaly is responsible for ice skating and glacial movement. JAMB tests this as a rare exception.
Question 31 of 40
In qualitative analysis, a white precipitate forms when dilute HCl is added to an unknown solution. The precipitate dissolves in excess ammonia solution. The cation present is most likely
Show Answer & Explanation
✓ Correct answer: B — Ag⁺
AgCl (white precipitate with HCl) dissolves in excess NH₃ to form the diamminesilver(I) complex: AgCl + 2NH₃ → [Ag(NH₃)₂]⁺ + Cl⁻. Question 32 of 40
The reaction between sodium thiosulphate and hydrochloric acid produces a yellow precipitate of sulphur. If the experiment is repeated with INCREASED concentration of HCl at the same temperature, which observation and conclusion is CORRECT?
Show Answer & Explanation
✓ Correct answer: B — The precipitate forms MORE QUICKLY; increased concentration increases collision frequency
Na₂S₂O₃ + 2HCl → 2NaCl + SO₂ + S↓ + H₂O. The yellow sulfur precipitate makes the solution cloudy. Question 33 of 40
From the concentration-time graph, which statement CORRECTLY distinguishes the effect of a catalyst (dashed curve) from increasing temperature (green curve)?
Show Answer & Explanation
✓ Correct answer: C — Temperature increases rate AND changes the equilibrium position; a catalyst only increases rate without changing equilibrium position
A catalyst speeds up BOTH forward and reverse reactions equally → rate increases but equilibrium position (and Kc) is UNCHANGED. Temperature changes the rate by providing more molecules with energy ≥ Eₐ, BUT temperature also affects Kc (via van't Hoff equation) → it DOES shift equilibrium position for exo/endothermic reactions. The graph shows: all curves reach the same final concentration — the catalyst reaches it fastest. If temperature affected equilibrium position, the green curve would reach a different final value. Option B captures the key distinction.

Question 37 of 40
The greenhouse effect involves infrared radiation being absorbed and re-emitted by atmospheric gases. Which of the following is NOT a greenhouse gas?
Show Answer & Explanation
✓ Correct answer: C — N₂
N₂ (dinitrogen) is a homonuclear diatomic molecule with no permanent dipole moment and no change in dipole moment during vibration → it cannot absorb infrared radiation → NOT a greenhouse gas. CO₂, CH₄, H₂O, N₂O, and O₃ all have asymmetric vibrations that absorb IR → they are greenhouse gases. N₂ and O₂ are the two major atmospheric components that do NOT contribute to the greenhouse effect.
Question 38 of 40
In the Haber process for ammonia synthesis, the iron catalyst is described as a heterogeneous catalyst. This means
Show Answer & Explanation
✓ Correct answer: A — The catalyst is in a different physical state from the reactants
A heterogeneous catalyst exists in a DIFFERENT phase from the reactants. In the Haber process: Fe catalyst is solid; N₂ and H₂ reactants are gases → different phases → heterogeneous. A homogeneous catalyst is in the same phase as reactants (e.g. conc. H₂SO₄ in esterification, both liquid). Catalysts are not consumed (regenerated). The Fe catalyst in Haber is promoted with K₂O and Al₂O₃.
Question 39 of 40
Which of the following aqueous solutions has the HIGHEST boiling point? [Assume all solutions are at the same concentration of 0.1 mol/dm³]
Show Answer & Explanation
✓ Correct answer: C — CaCl₂
Boiling point elevation ΔTb = iKbm, where i = van't Hoff factor (number of particles in solution). Glucose: i=1 (molecular, does not dissociate). Sucrose: i=1. NaCl: i≈2 (Na⁺ + Cl⁻). CaCl₂: i≈3 (Ca²⁺ + 2Cl⁻). Same molality → higher i = greater boiling point elevation. CaCl₂ has the highest i=3 → highest boiling point. This is one of the most elegant colligative property problems JAMB tests with electrolytes vs non-electrolytes.
Question 40 of 40
A student tests an unknown gas with a glowing splint and the splint RELIGHTS. The gas also turns limewater milky when bubbled through it. The gas is
Show Answer & Explanation
✓ Correct answer: B — A mixture of O₂ (relights splint) and CO₂ (turns limewater milky)
Relighting a glowing splint = test for O₂ (oxygen supports combustion). Turning limewater milky = test for CO₂ (Ca(OH)₂ + CO₂ → CaCO₃↓ + H₂O). A single pure gas cannot do BOTH. Pure O₂ relights a splint but does NOT turn limewater milky. Pure CO₂ turns limewater milky but EXTINGUISHES a glowing splint (it is not a supporter of combustion). Therefore, the sample must be a MIXTURE of both. This is a classic JAMB "two observations → two gases" identification trap.
