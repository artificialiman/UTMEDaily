# NUCLEAR CHEMISTRY

Atom named three routes out of the gate: isotope, ion, molecule. Ion and molecule got entire chapters. Isotope got a single line. This chapter is that line, expanded — and it's the one route where the nucleus itself, not the electrons, is doing all the work.

Everything before this chapter was electron chemistry. The currency was always electrons changing hands, protons never moving. Nuclear chemistry is the one place in this book where that founding rule is suspended — here, the nucleus itself is unstable, and the currency is mass and energy, not electrons.

---

## I. THE GATE

```
GATE → Is the nucleus stable?
   Stable    → ordinary isotope, sits quietly, never decays
   Unstable  → radioactive, decays — releasing particles/energy until it reaches a stable configuration
```

Instability isn't random. It's a ratio problem: nuclei with too many neutrons relative to protons (or too few, or simply too large overall, past around 82 protons) sit in an unstable arrangement and decay until the ratio corrects itself.

---

## II. TYPES OF DECAY

| Type | What's emitted | Mass number change | Atomic number change | Penetrating power |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Alpha (α) | Helium nucleus (2p + 2n) | −4 | −2 | Lowest — stopped by paper or skin |
| Beta (β) | High-speed electron (a neutron converts to a proton + electron) | 0 | +1 | Medium — stopped by a few mm of aluminum |
| Gamma (γ) | Pure energy (electromagnetic radiation, no mass, no charge) | 0 | 0 | Highest — needs thick lead or concrete to stop |

```
Alpha decay:  ᴬZX → ᴬ⁻⁴Z₋₂Y + ⁴₂He
Beta decay:   ᴬZX → ᴬZ₊₁Y + ⁰₋₁e
Gamma decay:  no change in identity — the nucleus just sheds excess energy after an alpha or beta emission
```

Relationship one-liner: **alpha decay is the nucleus throwing away mass to fix a ratio that's too large overall; beta decay is the nucleus converting a neutron into a proton internally to fix a ratio that's too neutron-heavy. Gamma is neither — it's just the leftover energy from either process, radiating away because the nucleus still has excess energy to shed after rearranging.**

This is also where Atom's mass number equation (nucleus = proton + neutron) gets its first real test: every decay equation above is that same equation, balanced on both sides, mass number and atomic number conserved exactly the way charge and mass are conserved in every other equation this book has written.

## III. HALF-LIFE — the constant that makes decay predictable despite being random

No single atom's decay moment can be predicted. A large enough sample's decay rate can be predicted with total precision — that gap between individual unpredictability and population predictability is the entire reason half-life is a usable constant at all.

```
Half-life (t½) = time for exactly half of a radioactive sample to decay
Amount remaining = initial amount × (1/2)^(number of half-lives elapsed)
```

| Isotope | Half-life | Common use |
|---|---|---|
| Carbon-14 | 5,730 years | Dating organic remains |
| Uranium-238 | 4.5 billion years | Dating rocks, geological time |
| Cobalt-60 | 5.3 years | Medical radiotherapy |
| Iodine-131 | 8 days | Medical imaging/treatment of the thyroid |

Carbon dating is Section II's decay equation read as a clock: a living organism keeps a constant ratio of C-14 to C-12 while alive (still exchanging carbon with its environment); the moment it dies, that exchange stops, and the C-14 already present starts decaying on a fixed schedule. Measuring how much C-14 is left, against how much a similar living sample would have, tells you how many half-lives have passed since the exchange stopped — not by direct observation of the death, but by reading a clock that started running at that exact moment.

## IV. FISSION AND FUSION — splitting versus combining, at the nucleus

| Process | What happens | Energy released | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| Fission | A large, unstable nucleus splits into two smaller nuclei | Very large — powers nuclear reactors and weapons | U-235 + neutron → Ba-141 + Kr-92 + 3 neutrons |
| Fusion | Two small nuclei combine into one larger nucleus | Even larger per reaction than fission — but needs extreme temperature/pressure to force nuclei close enough to fuse | Hydrogen isotopes fusing to helium — the Sun's actual power source |

Relationship one-liner: **fission and fusion sit at opposite ends of nuclear stability for the same reason atomic radius trends run opposite directions across the Periodic Table (Bonding & Periodicity, Section I) — very large nuclei are unstable and gain stability by splitting apart; very small nuclei are also relatively unstable and gain stability by combining. Iron sits at the most stable point in between, which is exactly why fusion stops producing net energy once it reaches iron, and why no natural stellar process fuses past it.**

The neutron released in a fission equation isn't incidental — it's what makes a chain reaction possible at all: one fission event's spare neutron can trigger the next nucleus's fission, the same way one displacement reaction's freed electron in Section VI of Bonding & Periodicity doesn't disappear, it goes on to do the next reaction's work.

## ONE LAST HALF-LIFE

Not a hierarchy diagram this time. A half-life doesn't announce itself — it just keeps halving, quietly, whether or not anyone's counting. So close this chapter the way the subject actually behaves:

Start with everything this book has told you an atom is: proton, neutron, electron, currency, gate. Halve it — keep only the nucleus. Halve it again — keep only the ratio of protons to neutrons. Halve it again — keep only whether that ratio is stable or not. One more halving, and you're left with a single number: t½, the time it takes for exactly half of whatever's left to disappear.

That number is the whole chapter. Not because nuclear chemistry is small — because every idea in it, alpha, beta, gamma, fission, fusion, was really just an answer to what a nucleus does about instability, and half-life is the constant that tells you how long it takes to do it.

Isotope was Atom's quietest route. It just spent an entire chapter proving it was never the quiet one at all.
